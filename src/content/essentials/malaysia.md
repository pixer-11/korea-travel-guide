**Quick answer:** Most Western, EU, and many Asian passport holders can enter Malaysia visa-free for 30–90 days but must submit the free Malaysia Digital Arrival Card (MDAC) online within 3 days of arrival; get around via Grab or the KL rail network (Touch 'n Go card), pay in Malaysian Ringgit (MYR), and dial 999 (or 112 from a mobile) in any emergency.

## Visa & entry

Malaysia's entry rules depend heavily on your passport, so treat the summary below as a starting point only. Citizens of the United States, United Kingdom, European Union member states, Japan, Australia, and Canada enter Malaysia without a visa for up to 90 days. Indian and Chinese nationals enjoy a 30-day visa exemption extended through 31 December 2026. Overall, Malaysia visa requirements in 2026 depend entirely on your nationality — over 130 countries enter visa-free for 30 or 90 days, while others must apply for an eVisa online before travel.

Whatever your nationality, almost everyone now needs to complete the **Malaysia Digital Arrival Card (MDAC)**. The Malaysia Digital Arrival Card (MDAC) is an official electronic form required of most foreign travellers to Malaysia, administered by the Immigration Department of Malaysia, and became mandatory from 1 January 2024 to streamline border processing, reduce wait times, and enhance security. It must be submitted through the official portal at imigresen-online.imi.gov.my/mdac/main within 3 days prior to arrival. MDAC is completely free of charge (RM 0); any website or service that charges a fee for MDAC submission is an unauthorized third party — avoid them, as the only legitimate portal is imigresen-online.imi.gov.my/mdac/main, operated directly by JIM. A short list of travelers are exempt, including citizens of Singapore, Malaysian citizens and permanent residents, diplomats and official passport holders, Bruneians with General Certificate of Identity, users of the Brunei–Malaysia Frequent Traveller Facility, Thai border-pass holders, and holders of Indonesian cross-border travel documents (PLB).

Also be aware that global pre-departure screening is expanding rapidly in 2026: as of April 2026, airlines in Malaysia can deny boarding on the spot, as visa-free travelers must complete pre-arrival forms or present an ETA QR code upon boarding. Practical tips: a valid passport (minimum 6 months validity from arrival date) plus flight details are required for the MDAC form. **Because visa-free lists, stay durations, and digital-entry requirements can change with little notice, always confirm your specific nationality's rules on the Malaysian Immigration Department website (imi.gov.my) before booking.**

## Getting around

Kuala Lumpur and other major cities have solid, affordable public transport, but the payment system has shifted heavily toward cashless in 2026. Kuala Lumpur's public transport includes an MRT (Mass Rapid Transit) system with three lines, an LRT (Light Rail Transit) system comprising three lines (Ampang, Sri Petaling, and Kelana Jaya), two Monorail lines, KTM Komuter train services connecting the suburbs to the city centre, a BRT (Bus Rapid Transit) system, and the ERL (Express Rail Link) connecting the city centre to KLIA airport.

For payment, the **Touch 'n Go card** is essential. City buses (Rapid KL, Go KL) do not accept cash or cards — the only way to pay is the Touch 'n Go transport card, which is a universal contactless card for all transport except taxis and the airport express. Card users should note that paying for public transport in Kuala Lumpur has become more difficult for tourists in 2026, as almost the entire system has switched to cashless payments and UnionPay cards are not accepted anywhere except the airport express ERL. For metro tickets specifically, at ticket machines for the MRT, LRT, and monorail, you can buy a single ticket using cash or Visa/Mastercard (except UnionPay). If you plan to stay a while, look into rail passes — Prasarana's PULSE pass gives unlimited rides within defined zones for a fixed monthly fee, and if you commute 5 days a week you'd otherwise spend roughly RM120 per month without a pass versus RM100 for the PULSE Rail pass. Ride-hailing apps like Grab are widely used and generally more convenient than street taxis for visitors. For journey planning, use Google Maps, Moovit, or transit apps to enter your starting point and destination, and the system will suggest the best route, including transfers if needed.

## Money & costs

Malaysia's currency is the Malaysian Ringgit (MYR, RM). Cards are widely accepted in cities, malls, and mid-to-upscale restaurants, but smaller eateries, markets, and rural areas often remain cash-only, so carry some ringgit at all times — especially small notes for transport ticket machines, which may not work with some banknotes, so it is better to have small change. ATMs are plentiful in urban areas; note that some payment networks (notably UnionPay) have limited acceptance on public transport. Overall, Malaysia remains a relatively budget-friendly destination for food, local transport, and accommodation compared to many Western countries, though prices rise in tourist hotspots like KLCC, Langkawi resorts, and during peak holiday seasons.

## Best time to visit

Malaysia is tropical and hot-and-humid year-round, but rainfall patterns differ sharply by coast, so timing depends on where you're headed. The months between March and October are considered the best time to visit the country, characterised by mostly dry weather. More specifically, the west coast (Kuala Lumpur, Penang, Langkawi) experiences its wet season from April to October, while the east coast (Perhentian Islands, Tioman, Kota Bharu) gets heavy rains from November to February.

For beach and island trips on the east coast, the monsoon arrives in Malaysia between September and February, and boat and ferry trips to and from the islands can be delayed for hours or cancelled altogether due to strong winds and stormy seas, with murky seawater ruling out diving and snorkelling — so this may not be the best time to travel if you're here on a beach or island vacation. Conversely, west-coast destinations like Kuala Lumpur, Penang, and Langkawi are typically drier and sunnier from around December to March. If heading to the highlands, pack layers: the only exceptions to hot and humid Malaysian weather are the country's highlands and mountain ranges — for example, in peninsular Malaysia, areas like the Cameron Highlands stay around the mid 20°Cs and can drop below 60°F (15°C) during the winter months. Bear in mind that Malaysia has two peak seasons — winter (December–February) and summer (June–August) — with a huge footfall due to school vacations and favourable weather, meaning higher prices during these months.

## Emergencies & safety

Malaysia uses a unified emergency number, making it simple to remember: Malaysia's national emergency number works from any phone — landline or mobile — for police, ambulance, or fire, dial 999; from a mobile phone, 112 also works, even without a SIM card. This system was introduced on 1 October 2007, combining all emergency numbers namely 991, 994 and 999 into one emergency number involving the Malaysian Civil Defense Department (JPAM), the Royal Malaysian Police (PDRM), the Ministry of Health, and the Fire and Rescue Department, and every call through the 999 emergency line is free.

A dedicated fire line also exists (994), and the UK Foreign Office confirms the same core numbers for visitors: Police and ambulance: 999, Fire: 994, Ambulance (from a mobile): 112. Many cities also run a Tourist Police service for lost property, theft reports, and general assistance in tourist zones, with officers commonly stationed in busy areas of Kuala Lumpur such as Bukit Bintang, KLCC and Chinatown, where they can help with lost property, reports and general assistance in tourist zones — but for any genuine emergency, always call 999 first.

On healthcare costs, note that while Malaysia doesn't require travel insurance for entry, tourists must pay for medical treatment at the time of service, with private hospital stays costing around USD $50/night for a basic room and complex procedures costing USD $7,000–$8,000 or more — so comprehensive travel insurance is strongly recommended. Common tourist-related crime is generally low-level: crimes against tourists are typically more minor, with common crimes being pickpocketing, burglaries, credit card fraud and vehicle theft being the most frequent. As with any destination, check your own government's latest travel advisory before departure, since conditions and regional warnings can change.
