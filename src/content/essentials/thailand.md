**Quick answer:** Most nationalities can still enter Thailand visa-free (currently up to 60 days for many passports, though a Cabinet-approved cut to 30 days is pending and could take effect any time), everyone must file a free online Thailand Digital Arrival Card (TDAC) before arrival, the baht is the only currency to pay in, and November–February offers the coolest, driest weather countrywide.

## Visa & entry

Thailand currently allows nationals of many countries — commonly cited as around 93 countries and territories — to enter without a pre-arranged visa. Following a major policy shift that began in mid-2024, the standard stay for visa-exempt travellers was extended from 30 days to 60 days, a change confirmed and maintained into 2026 that applies to the majority of the 93 eligible countries. Some nationalities have different arrangements — for example, South Korea receives 90 days under a bilateral treaty, Japan receives 30 days, China gets 60 days, while most ASEAN member states such as Malaysia, Indonesia, Vietnam, the Philippines, and Cambodia receive 30 days visa-free under regional agreements.

**Important — this is changing.** Thailand's Cabinet approved the end of the 60-day visa-free scheme on May 19, 2026, reverting most nationalities to 30 days, with the change taking effect 15 days after publication in the Royal Gazette. Under the new framework, the visa-free stay is reduced to 30 days for many travelers, while certain nationalities now qualify for stays of only 15 days, and visa-on-arrival eligibility now applies to fewer countries. As of this writing, Thailand's official consular information continued to show visa-free stays of up to 60 days for travellers from 93 eligible countries and territories, but the timing of the switch depends entirely on Royal Gazette publication — **do not assume either 60 or 30 days applies to you; verify your exact nationality's allowance on the official Ministry of Foreign Affairs/embassy site within days of booking and again before departure.**

Regardless of visa category, Thailand requires all foreign nationals to complete the Thailand Digital Arrival Card (TDAC) before arrival, and it is now required for all foreign nationals entering Thailand by air, land, or sea. Key practical points:
- Submit between 24 and 72 hours before your arrival — submitting earlier than 72 hours will be rejected.
- Completing the TDAC is free; if you encounter a website charging a fee, you are not on the official portal.
- A new TDAC is needed for every entry — it is valid for a single entry only, and a new one must be submitted every time you cross the border, including short visa runs to neighbouring countries.

Also note: proof of sufficient funds (10,000 THB per person or 20,000 THB per family) may be checked at the officer's discretion, and a single 30-day extension is available at a local immigration office for 1,900 THB, subject to officer approval. An extension only applies to the visa-exempt entry, not indefinitely.

## Getting around

Bangkok's rail network is split across a few operators, but paying is easier than ever for visitors. Bangkok's MRT system fully transitioned to EMV Contactless payments starting June 1, 2026, making old stored-value cards obsolete on the Blue and Purple Lines, so commuters can now tap standard contactless credit/debit cards (Visa, Mastercard, UnionPay) or a new dedicated Mangmoom EMV card. For the BTS Skytrain, the Rabbit Card remains valid only on the BTS network, and for MRT rides you need a separate ticket, token, or compatible contactless payment card — though the Rabbit card is also used on the Pink and Yellow monorail lines, and since 2025 the Mangmoom Card has allowed travel across all MRT-operated lines (Blue, Purple, Pink, Yellow), though that Mangmoom card is largely for Thai ID holders. The simplest strategy for tourists: **carry one contactless Visa/Mastercard** — the MRT now accepts contactless credit and debit cards (Visa and Mastercard) directly at the turnstiles, removing the need for a specific MRT card, and it also works at many BTS gates. Just confirm your home bank has enabled international contactless payments and travel notifications before you fly.

Beyond rail, Bangkok and most tourist hubs are well served by metered taxis, ride-hailing apps (Grab is dominant), tuk-tuks (agree the fare first), and long-distance buses/trains connecting to Chiang Mai, the islands, and the border regions. Domestic flights are cheap and frequent for longer hops (e.g., Bangkok–Phuket, Bangkok–Chiang Mai).

## Money & costs

The currency is the Thai baht (THB, ฿), divided into 100 satang, with one US dollar buying roughly ฿32–33 as of mid-2026. Thailand is still a cash-first country outside hotels and malls — street food, taxis, markets, and temple entry are cash-only — but every tourist area is dense with ATMs and exchange booths.

ATM fees for foreign cards have crept up: in 2026 some ATMs now display higher fees, including 250 baht with Visa and up to 350 baht with some foreign Mastercard cards, on top of whatever your home bank charges. Two golden rules: charge in baht, never your home currency, and withdraw big, rarely — the ATM fee is fixed whether you take ฿1,000 or ฿25,000. Always decline "dynamic currency conversion" offers at ATMs and card terminals; if the ATM offers to convert the amount into euros, dollars or pounds, choose to be charged in Thai baht instead.

Cards are widely accepted in malls, chain restaurants, and hotels in cities, but keep small baht notes for markets, tuk-tuks, and rural areas. A useful budgeting benchmark: about ฿3,200–3,300 covers 2–3 days of comfortable budget travel — a guesthouse night, three street-food or local-restaurant meals, local transport, and a massage, with change left over.

## Best time to visit

Thailand has three broad seasons, and timing depends on your priorities. For the most reliable overall weather, aim for December to February; Thailand's rainy season usually runs from June to October across many regions, although the timing varies by coast. November to February is Thailand's peak tourist period, with daytime temperatures typically between the low 20s and low 30s °C, lower humidity, and clear skies across most of the country — but expect the highest hotel prices and biggest crowds at major sites.

March–May brings the hot season, when temperatures can make things somewhat uncomfortable nationwide, especially inland in Bangkok and Chiang Mai; note also that air quality can drop in March and April due to agricultural burning, particularly in the north. If budget and smaller crowds matter more than guaranteed sunshine, June to August is Thailand's low season, when hotel rates can drop by up to 50%, and many tour operators offer significant discounts, with rain typically arriving as short, heavy afternoon downpours rather than all-day washouts. Coastal timing varies too — the Andaman Coast, including Phuket and Krabi, is often wetter from May to October, while parts of the Gulf Coast, including Koh Samui, can follow a different pattern.

## Emergencies & safety

Save these numbers before you land:

| Service | Number |
|---|---|
| Tourist Police (English-speaking, 24/7) | **1155** |
| General police emergency | **191** |
| Medical emergency / ambulance | **1669** |
| Fire | **199** |
| Highway Police (road accidents) | **1193** |
| Tourism Authority of Thailand hotline | **1672** |

In Thailand there's no single number that does everything — Thailand has made 191 the main call center for all emergencies, but the operator may not speak English, so if you can't communicate, hang up and call 1155 (Tourist Police) instead, as they'll translate and dispatch for you. The Tourist Police can help with theft, scams, taxi/tuk-tuk disputes, and accompanying you to file an official police report, which you'll need for insurance claims.

General safety: Thailand is largely safe for tourists, but stay alert for common scams (gem/tailor scams, jet-ski damage claims, overcharging), wear a helmet on rented motorbikes (and check your travel insurance covers this — many policies exclude motorbike injuries without a valid local license), and drink bottled/filtered water. Carry your passport or a copy at all times, as ID checks do happen. For medical issues, Thailand's private hospitals in Bangkok, Phuket, and Chiang Mai offer high-quality care; make sure your travel insurance includes medical evacuation coverage and keep the insurer's 24-hour emergency line saved separately from local numbers.
