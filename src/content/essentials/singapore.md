**Quick answer:** Most Western passport holders get visa-free entry for social/business visits (commonly up to 30–90 days depending on nationality), but everyone must submit a free digital SG Arrival Card before landing, tap in with a contactless card on the excellent MRT/bus network, and always verify current rules on official ICA and LTA sites before you fly.

## Visa & entry

Whether you need a visa depends entirely on your passport. Singapore's Immigration & Checkpoints Authority (ICA) maintains a list of nationalities that require a visa to enter — Afghanistan, Algeria, Armenia, Azerbaijan, Bangladesh, Belarus, India, Iran, Iraq, Pakistan, Russia, and several other countries are on that list, while most Western, ASEAN, and many other passport holders enter visa-free. According to one 2026 traveler guide, UK, Australian, American, Canadian, German, Japanese, and South Korean citizens all get three months visa-free, though exact durations vary by nationality and can change without notice.

Regardless of visa status, nearly everyone must complete the **SG Arrival Card (SGAC)** before arrival. The SG Arrival Card is a digital entry requirement used by most travellers arriving in Singapore, administered by the Immigration and Checkpoints Authority, which replaced the former paper-based disembarkation/embarkation card system. Key facts to know:

- You are required to submit the SGAC within three (3) days (including the day of arrival) before arrival in Singapore.
- The SG Arrival Card is not a visa. If your nationality requires a visa, you must apply for that separately through ICA or an overseas mission.
- The SGAC is valid for a single entry into Singapore — you must submit a new one every time you enter, even for a day trip from Malaysia.
- Submission is free through ICA's official SG Arrival Card e-Service or the MyICA Mobile app — never pay a third-party site.
- Since early 2026, enforcement has tightened: from 30 January 2026, ICA may issue No-Boarding Directives to airlines for travellers who are identified as prohibited, undesirable, or who do not meet Singapore's entry requirements, such as having a valid visa or a travel document with at least six months' validity.

Also confirm general entry basics: all travellers, except Singapore passport holders, must ensure their passport has a minimum validity of 6 months, and collective/group passports aren't accepted.

**Because entry rules, visa-exempt periods, and fees change, always confirm your specific requirements on the official ICA website before booking or flying.**

## Getting around

Singapore's MRT (metro) and bus network is clean, efficient, and fully air-conditioned, and it's now almost entirely cashless at the gates. Standard single-trip paper tickets and cash payments at the gantry are no longer accepted anywhere on the MRT network. Your simplest option as a visitor is to use what's already in your wallet: the fastest and easiest way is to simply tap your contactless bank card (Visa, Mastercard, AMEX, or NETS) or a mobile wallet (Apple Pay, Google Pay, Samsung Pay) directly at the fare gantry, with no prior registration needed. This system is called **SimplyGo**.

A few practical tips:
- Fares are distance-based; adult card fares range from about S$1.28 for short trips up to S$2.57 for the longest cross-island journeys, with cash fares costing about S$0.22 more per trip.
- One important gotcha: you must tap in and tap out with the exact same card or device — if you tap in with a phone and tap out with a physical card, or any mismatched combination, the system can't match the journey and you get charged the maximum possible fare on both cards.
- If you'd rather have a dedicated transit card, you can purchase a SimplyGo EZ-Link card from convenience stores or station ticket offices.
- Heavy sightseers can consider the **Singapore Tourist Pass**, offering unlimited daily rides for SGD 17/24/29 for 1/2/3 days unlimited — only worth it for high trip volumes.
- Trains generally run roughly 5:30am to midnight. Taxis and ride-hailing apps (Grab, Gojek) are widely available and metered/app-priced.

## Money & costs

Singapore's currency is the **Singapore Dollar (SGD/S$)**. Contactless card payments and mobile wallets are accepted almost everywhere — hawker centres, taxis, retail, and transit — making Singapore one of the most card-friendly cities in Asia; carrying some cash is still useful for smaller hawker stalls or wet markets that may prefer it. ATMs are widely available at malls, MRT stations, and the airport for cash withdrawals. Budget travelers can eat well cheaply at hawker centres, while dining, hotels, and attractions can be pricey relative to the region — expect Singapore's costs to sit closer to those of a major Western city than to its Southeast Asian neighbours. A Goods and Services Tax (GST) is typically already included in listed retail and dining prices; verify current GST rates and any tourist refund schemes with official sources before a shopping trip.

## Best time to visit

Singapore sits near the equator, so it's hot and humid year-round with daily highs staying around 30°C to 32°C all year, meaning the best time to visit depends more on rainfall than temperature. For the most comfortable sightseeing conditions, aim for February to April and June to September, when drier weather and lower humidity make sightseeing more comfortable. February in particular tends to be especially dry. Conversely, the Northeast Monsoon dumps heavy, prolonged rain from November through January, though most rain rolls in between 2:00 PM and 6:00 PM, so mornings are usually fine for outdoor plans even in wetter months. Bring an umbrella year-round regardless of season, and note that late-year visits coincide with festive Christmas lights on Orchard Road and, depending on the lunar calendar, Chinese New Year celebrations.

## Emergencies & safety

Singapore is very safe for visitors, with responsive, well-organized emergency services. Save these numbers before you arrive:

- **Police (crime in progress / life-threatening):** 999
- **Ambulance & Fire (life-threatening):** 995 — operated by the Singapore Civil Defence Force (SCDF)
- **Non-emergency ambulance:** 1777
- **Police SMS (if unsafe to call):** 70999 / 71999 (source variance — check both, as different guides cite these interchangeably for the deaf/hard-of-hearing and unsafe-to-talk services)
- **Police non-emergency hotline:** 1800 255 0000

Emergency calls are answered quickly, with police pledging to answer 999 calls within 10 seconds and reach your location within 15 minutes. Both emergency lines are free and staffed 24/7. The SCDF ambulance service is free of charge if used for genuine medical emergencies, though non-emergency use of 995 can incur a fee, so use 1777 for non-urgent medical transport. If you're an international traveler dialing from a foreign phone, note that 911 calls are routed automatically to 999 as police recognize some foreigners are used to calling 911 for an emergency.
