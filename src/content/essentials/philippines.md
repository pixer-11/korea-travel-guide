**Quick answer:** Most nationalities (including the US, UK, EU, Canada, Australia, and 150+ others) can enter the Philippines visa-free for up to 30 days, but always double-check your specific nationality's rules and complete the mandatory eTravel registration before arrival; the best months to visit are December–May (dry season), and the national emergency number is 911.

## Visa & entry

The Philippines runs one of the more open visa policies in Southeast Asia. The Philippines maintains a welcoming policy for over 150 countries, and under EO 408, nationals from qualified countries can enter for tourism or business purposes without a visa for an initial stay of up to 30 days. Two notable 2025–2026 changes affect major source markets: India now gets 14-day visa-free entry (effective 8 June 2025), conditional on tourism/business purpose, a passport valid 6+ months, confirmed accommodation, proof of funds, and a return ticket, and effective 16 January 2026, Chinese nationals may enter without a visa for tourism or business for a non-extendable 14-day stay, non-convertible to any other visa category.

Regardless of nationality, everyone should be ready with the same baseline documents: a passport valid at least six months beyond your intended stay, and a confirmed return/onward ticket departing within your authorized stay period. All passengers must also register via the eTravel.gov.ph portal before or on arrival. Airlines take the onward-ticket rule seriously — passengers are regularly denied boarding if they cannot present an onward ticket, even if otherwise eligible to enter. If you want to stay longer, you can typically file your first extension online around day 25, and staying beyond 59 days usually triggers ACR I-Card requirements for tourists. A separate e-Visa channel is also expanding for additional markets. Because entry rules change frequently (and some nationalities still require a visa in advance), **always confirm your specific requirements on the official Bureau of Immigration and e-Visa/eTravel portals before you fly** — final entry is always at the discretion of the immigration officer at the border.

## Getting around

Domestic flights are the practical way to hop between major islands (Manila, Cebu, Palawan, Boracay's Caticlan/Kalibo, Siargao, Davao), supplemented by inter-island ferries for shorter Visayas hops.

Within cities, options include:
- **Grab** – the dominant ride-hailing app since Uber exited the market; Grab lets you book either a private car or a metered taxi with the fare agreed up front, saving you from haggling.
- **Jeepneys** – the classic, ultra-cheap local ride; fares start around ₱13 for short city trips, with routes marked by a code of letters and numbers on the front. Newer air-conditioned "modern jeepneys" now accept tap cards.
- **Tricycles** – motorized sidecars for short, local trips; negotiate the fare before boarding, especially at ports and terminals.
- **Trains (Metro Manila)** – LRT-1, LRT-2, and MRT-3 crisscross the capital, with fares in the ₱15–₱30 range. The beep card is the Philippines' unified contactless stored-value card for public transit — one tap-and-go card works across LRT-1, LRT-2, MRT-3, the EDSA Carousel, and most P2P bus routes. Beep cards are available at all MRT/LRT ticket booths, PITX, One Ayala, and select 7-Eleven branches, and can be reloaded via GCash or Maya apps.
- **City buses** – e.g., Cebu's MyBus network, with an air-conditioned bus network linking key spots like SM City, SM Seaside, IT Park and the airport, with fares roughly ₱25 to ₱50 depending on distance.

Carry small cash for jeepneys and tricycles in case card systems are down, and expect chaotic but cheap and colorful transport overall.

## Money & costs

The currency is the Philippine peso (PHP). You can easily exchange currencies at banks and currency exchange bureaus, and ATMs are widely available in cities and tourist destinations — but it's wise to always carry some cash, particularly in smaller towns and remote islands. Credit/debit cards are accepted at hotels, malls, and larger restaurants in cities, but many jeepneys, tricycles, sari-sari stores, and rural vendors are cash-only, so keep small peso bills on hand. Budget travel is very achievable: hostel dorms and simple guesthouses can run a few hundred pesos a night in beach towns, while mid-range resorts and city hotels cost more, and street food/local "carinderia" meals are inexpensive. As a rule of thumb, notify your bank of travel plans, use ATMs at reputable banks rather than standalone machines, and keep a small emergency cash reserve for when digital payment systems or signal drop out.

## Best time to visit

The Philippines has a tropical climate split broadly into a dry and wet season. The dry season (November to April) is considered the best time to visit due to more predictable weather, while the country experiences heavy rainfall, particularly during the rainy season from May to October. Within the dry season, the best months to visit are December, January, and February — while this period is winter, temperatures remain warm and comfortable, making it the most pleasant time of year for travellers. March to May is the Philippines' summer season — the hottest and driest months of the year — perfect for sunshine and swimming, though inland areas can get quite warm.

Typhoon season runs roughly June through November, with the rainy season (June to October) also being the main typhoon season, especially from August onward, and storms causing flight and ferry cancellations, particularly in Luzon and the Visayas. Some regions are less exposed: Palawan and the southern Visayas are less likely to experience typhoons. If you travel in shoulder or wet months, build flexibility into your itinerary and monitor PAGASA weather advisories.

## Emergencies & safety

911 is the nationwide emergency number, working across the whole country for police, fire and medical emergencies, connecting you to the National Emergency Hotline. In September 2025, the DILG rolled out the Unified 911, a centralised emergency hotline that brings together local emergency contact numbers from across the country, integrating police, fire, and hospital services into one system. The Philippine National Police can also be reached on the Philippine National Police Hotline: 117 or (02) 8722-0650. For sea-related incidents, call the Philippine Coast Guard, and before any crossing, check PAGASA storm signals — when ferries are suspended, stay put rather than chancing rough water.

On overall safety, the Philippines is a safe and friendly destination for the vast majority of travellers — headline tourist regions like Palawan, Cebu, Bohol, Boracay, and Siargao see millions of visitors a year and call for nothing more than everyday common sense: watch your belongings in crowds, avoid flashing valuables, and use registered transport at night. The main genuine exception is parts of western Mindanao around the Sulu archipelago, where many governments maintain travel advisories — well away from the standard tourist trail. If you're a victim of theft, report it to the local police (or the Tourist Police where available) for a report your insurer will need. Note also that the Philippines does not participate in broad reciprocal healthcare arrangements comparable to the EU's EHIC system or Australasia's reciprocal health agreements, so travel insurance with medical coverage is strongly recommended.
