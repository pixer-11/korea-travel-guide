**Quick answer:** Most Western passport holders can still enter Japan visa-free for up to 90 days in 2026 (no ETA/JESTA required yet — that system isn't launching until fiscal 2028), IC cards (Suica/Pasmo/ICOCA) make transport effortless, and Japan remains a cash-friendly but increasingly cashless, very safe destination — just confirm the latest entry rules before you fly since they can change quickly.

## Visa & entry

To enter Japan in 2026 as a tourist, most travelers from visa-exempt countries need a valid passport, a completed registration on the Visit Japan Web portal (to obtain Immigration and Customs QR codes), and a confirmed onward or return ticket, with stays typically granted for up to 90 days. Japan maintains visa exemption agreements with over 70 countries and regions worldwide, including the United States, Canada, Australia, the United Kingdom, and most of Europe. Travelers who plan to stay longer than 90 days, take up paid employment, study, or participate in long-term activities must apply for the appropriate visa before traveling.

A widely discussed change is **JESTA** (Japan Electronic System for Travel Authorization), Japan's planned equivalent of the US ESTA. Importantly, the law establishing it passed on May 29, 2026, but the system launches no later than March 2029 (fiscal 2028) — you do not need JESTA for 2026 or 2027 travel. As of early 2026, no pilot programme has launched and no official JESTA application portal exists, so be wary of third-party sites claiming to offer "early registration." Japan lifted all COVID-19-related entry requirements, including vaccination certificates and pre-departure testing, back in 2023, and you do not need to show any proof of vaccination to enter in 2026.

**Because entry rules change, always verify your specific requirements on the official links in the Sources section below before booking or flying** — do not rely solely on this or any other unofficial guide.

## Getting around

Japan's rail network is the backbone of getting around, and for city travel a rechargeable **IC card** is essential. Suica, Pasmo and ICOCA are interoperable across Japan and work on most trains, buses and shops — the ones tourists meet most often are Suica (JR East), Pasmo (Tokyo private rail and subway operators), and ICOCA (JR West/Osaka). Visitors can buy a tourist-specific version: the Welcome Suica Card is a prepaid contactless IC card designed for short-term visitors, valid for 28 days from first use, with no deposit required and a maximum balance of ¥20,000. A **Tourist PASMO** works similarly and launched on 20 May 2026, replacing the previous PASMO PASSPORT program. iPhone users have an even easier option: Welcome Suica Mobile is a free, iPhone-only digital tourist card from JR East — 180-day validity, no deposit, no airport counter line.

For long-distance travel, the **JR Pass** covers Shinkansen (bullet train) and other JR services, but buying a JR Pass for a single-city trip usually isn't worth it — for Tokyo-only travel, an IC card and local tickets are typically cheaper, so calculate your expected train spend before deciding. Keep in mind that most Shinkansen journeys need a separate ticket or pass even if you have an IC card. Buses and taxis nationwide also accept IC cards, and for a typical tourist day in Tokyo or Osaka you'll tap an IC card 10 to 20 times.

## Money & costs

The currency is the **Japanese yen (JPY)**, and while Japan is going increasingly cashless, cash still matters. Cashless payments reached 58.0% of consumer spending in 2025, and in the big cities you can tap a card or phone almost everywhere, but cash is still essential at small family-run restaurants, temples and shrines, market stalls, festival food carts, older ryokan, and off the tourist trail.

For withdrawing cash, the best place is Seven Bank ATMs in 7-Eleven and Japan Post ATMs, which accept foreign cards, run 24/7, with English menus and a 100,000 yen per-withdrawal cap. Specifically, Seven Bank's 28,000+ ATMs accept Visa, Plus, Mastercard, Maestro, Cirrus, UnionPay, American Express and JCB cards issued abroad, run 24 hours a day, offer multilingual menus, and cap each withdrawal at 100,000 yen. A few practical tips: if offered a choice between your home currency and JPY, always choose JPY — the DCC (Dynamic Currency Conversion) rate is set by the bank and is typically 3–5% worse. Also, tell your bank you'll be traveling in Japan so it doesn't freeze your card on the first foreign transaction, and make sure you know your PIN, since Japanese ATMs typically use standard numeric PINs, and if your card has a PIN longer than 4 digits it may not work. For cards, Visa/Mastercard have the widest acceptance, JCB is good in Japan, while Amex is patchy — carry a main Visa/MC plus a backup JCB if possible. Note also that tipping is not customary in Japan.

## Best time to visit

Japan is a year-round destination, but timing shapes your experience considerably. Pleasant temperatures and clear blue skies make late spring (March to May) and late autumn (October to November) the best seasons to visit Japan. Cherry blossoms bloom between late March and early April in most major cities, with northern regions like Hokkaido blooming later, typically mid to late April. These peak windows come at a cost: these are also the most crowded and most expensive periods, with accommodation in Kyoto during peak cherry blossom or koyo (foliage) season costing two to three times what it costs in January.

Summer brings its own challenges. Japan's rainy season (tsuyu) typically occurs from mid-June to mid-July, and July and August are hot and humid except in Hokkaido, with temperatures in Tokyo reaching up to 88°F. Late summer into early autumn also carries storm risk: in late summer it's not uncommon for typhoons to affect travel plans, such as flight cancellations to and from Okinawa, though this tends to happen less in most other parts of Japan. Budget-conscious travelers should note that January–February and parts of early summer tend to offer the lowest prices on hotels and flights.

## Emergencies & safety

Japan uses separate numbers for different emergencies rather than a single unified line. Ambulance is dialed at 119, Fire at 119, and Police at 110, and these apply nationwide. For maritime issues, Maritime Emergencies use 118 to contact the Japan Coast Guard. Importantly for tourists, all emergency calls (110, 119, 118) are free from any phone — including locked phones with no SIM, payphones, and hotel room phones.

Language can be a barrier: while English and a handful of other foreign languages are supported through a 3rd-party interpreter, this takes time. If you're unsure whether a situation needs an ambulance, Medical advice line #7119 is free with English available in Tokyo 24/7. For general tourist assistance in English, Chinese, and Korean, the Japan National Tourism Organization operates a 24/7 hotline at 050-3816-2787 for tourists. Small neighborhood **koban** (police boxes) near stations are useful for lost property and minor non-emergency issues. Overall, Japan has a strong reputation for safety, and natural-disaster preparedness (earthquakes, typhoons) is worth a quick read before you go — download the "Safety Tips" app for alerts in English.
