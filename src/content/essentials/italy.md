## Visa & entry

Italy is a full member of the Schengen Area. Citizens of 59 visa-exempt countries — including the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Japan, and most Latin American nations — can enter Italy without a visa for short stays of up to 90 days within any 180-day period. Importantly, your 90 days are counted across ALL Schengen countries combined — spending 30 days in France, 30 days in Italy, and 30 days in Spain uses your entire 90-day allowance. If your nationality isn't visa-exempt, you'll need to apply for a Schengen short-stay visa (Type C) through the Italian consulate in your home country before traveling, which allows stays of up to 90 days within a 180-day period and typically costs €80 for adults.

Two major EU border-control changes are underway in 2026. First, the **Entry/Exit System (EES)**, a biometric system that becomes operational in April 2026, automatically tracks the exact duration of every non-EU visitor's stay in the Schengen Area, replacing the outdated manual passport stamping system by registering biometric data like fingerprints and facial images at border crossings. Second, **ETIAS** (European Travel Information and Authorisation System) — confirmed by the European Commission for a Q4 2026 launch, most likely October or November 2026, with a transitional grace period following until roughly April 2027 before full enforcement begins across all Schengen borders. Once mandatory, the fee is 20 euros for adults aged 18 to 70, with children under 18 and seniors over 70 exempt, and the authorization remains valid for three years or until passport expiry, whichever comes first.

**Bottom line for July 2026 travel:** you can currently enter Italy visa-free with just a passport if you're from an exempt country, but keep your passport valid at least 3 months beyond your departure date, and **confirm the very latest ETIAS/EES status on the official EU and Italian government sites before you travel**, since the timeline has shifted before and enforcement details can change quickly.

## Getting around

Italy has an extensive, tourist-friendly transport network:

- **Trains** are the backbone of intercity travel. Trenitalia (state operator) and Italo (private high-speed operator) connect major cities like Rome, Florence, Milan, Venice, and Naples with frequent high-speed services. Book online or via app in advance for the best fares, especially for high-speed (Frecciarossa/Italo) routes.
- **City transit**: Rome, Milan, Naples, and other major cities run integrated metro/bus/tram networks. Rome and most cities sell single tickets, 24/48/72-hour passes, and weekly passes valid across metro, bus, and tram.
- **Contactless payment**: Many transit systems in major cities (Rome, Milan) now accept tap-to-pay debit/credit cards directly at metro gates, in addition to traditional paper tickets — convenient for short visits.
- **Taxis & rideshare**: Only use official white taxis with meters/rate cards at designated taxi stands or via phone/app booking; Uber operates in a limited, more expensive form in a few cities.
- **Driving**: A rental car is useful for the countryside (Tuscany, Puglia, the Dolomites) but avoid driving in city historic centers, which are typically restricted "ZTL" (Limited Traffic Zones) zones monitored by camera — fines for unauthorized entry are common and can arrive months later by mail.
- **Ferries**: Essential for islands (Sicily, Sardinia, Capri) and coastal towns like the Amalfi Coast and Cinque Terre.

## Money & costs

Italy's currency is the **euro (€)**. Italy uses the euro and offers a mix of modern card payments and very traditional cash habits. Practical guidance:

- **Cards**: Cards from VISA and Mastercard are accepted in Italy, especially in touristy establishments. Italy is quickly embracing contactless and digital payments, and many shops, hotels, and restaurants now accept Apple Pay, Google Pay, and Samsung Pay, though in smaller towns or local markets, cash is still king.
- **Cash**: Carrying €50–€100 in cash per day is usually enough for small expenses like coffee, transport, or local attractions. Some small cafés, markets, and taxis still prefer or require cash.
- **ATMs**: ATMs are usually available in cities and major towns, while rural areas can have fewer options; typical fees include a local ATM fee plus your bank's foreign transaction fee, so withdraw larger amounts less often to reduce fees. Always decline "dynamic currency conversion" and choose to be charged in euros.
- **Tipping**: There is no pressing need to tip at restaurants — touristy places might expect a tip from Americans, but those who do leave one usually give just a couple of euros, nowhere near US-style 20%. Look for "coperto" (cover charge) on the bill — if you see coperto on the bill, that covers service for the table.
- **Currency exchange**: Avoid airport exchange counters and street changers; use bank ATMs instead for better rates.

## Best time to visit

The nicest time to visit, in terms of the weather and lack of crowds, is from April to May, and in September or October — these shoulder months offer a perfect balance of pleasant weather and fewer tourists. Italy's best travel months are May, June, September, and October, though they're also the busiest and most expensive time to visit; crowds aside, these months combine the convenience of peak season with pleasant weather.

If you're traveling in July or August (peak summer), be prepared: the heat in July and August can be grueling, particularly in the south, where temperatures hit the 90s (°F). August is also when many Italians take their summer vacations; big cities tend to be quiet (with discounted hotel prices), but beach and mountain resorts are jammed (with higher hotel prices).

For a quieter, budget-friendly trip, between November and April you can expect cooler weather and you'll miss most of the sweat and stress of the tourist season, though off-season means shorter hours at sights and more lunchtime breaks. Regional variation matters too — Venice is magical in April–May and September–October, with 15–25°C (59–77°F) temperatures and fewer crowds, while summer sees heavy tourism, high humidity, and higher water levels.

## Emergencies & safety

If you find yourself in serious difficulty and need help, the single number to call for emergencies in Italy is 112 — a free number to dial without any prefix, and the operator will put you in touch with the most appropriate authority or service. Specific direct lines also exist:
