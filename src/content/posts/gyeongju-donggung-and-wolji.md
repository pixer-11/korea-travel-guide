## Why Go

Donggung and Wolji (formerly known as Anapji) was built in the 7th century during the Unified Silla period as a secondary palace and banquet site for royal receptions and to entertain visiting dignitaries. The complex fell into ruin over the centuries, and by the 20th century the pond had become an overgrown marsh known locally as Anapji ("goose and duck pond"). Excavations in the 1970s recovered tens of thousands of Silla-era artifacts — roof tiles, dice, wooden documents, and bronze items — many of which are now displayed at the Gyeongju National Museum. Three of the original more-than-twenty pavilions have been reconstructed along the pond's edge, and their lantern-lit reflections at night have made this one of the most photographed spots in Gyeongju, arguably rivaling Bulguksa temple for name recognition among international visitors.

## Getting There

Donggung and Wolji sits in the Wolseong-dong area of Gyeongju, within easy reach of the historic core. From Gyeongju Intercity Bus Terminal or Gyeongju Station, local city buses (look for routes running toward Bulguksa or the Tumuli Park/Cheomseongdae area, such as 10, 11, or 70-series buses common on tourist routes) stop within a short walk of the entrance; a taxi from either the bus terminal or the KTX Singyeongju Station takes roughly 10–20 minutes depending on traffic. Many visitors simply walk from Daereungwon (Tumuli Park) or Cheomseongdae, since the whole historic zone is flat and pedestrian-friendly — the walk from Cheomseongdae takes about 15–20 minutes. Renting a bicycle, widely available near the tumuli area, is a popular way to link these sites without waiting on buses.

## What to See

The main draw is the walk around the pond's irregular, deliberately non-symmetrical shoreline, designed to appear larger than it is and to hide the far bank from any single vantage point — a hallmark of Silla garden design. The three reconstructed pavilions (part of what was once a much larger palace complex used for state banquets) anchor the eastern side of the pond and are the classic photo spot, especially after sunset when they're illuminated and cast long reflections on the still water. Budget 45 minutes to an hour for a full loop; photographers often linger longer near the water's edge waiting for calm conditions. If time allows, cross over to the Gyeongju National Museum (a 10–15 minute walk or short drive away) to see the excavated artifacts and get context for what the palace grounds once contained — including the famous 14-sided wooden die used for drinking games, a small but memorable piece testifying to Silla court life.

## When to Go

Evening is the signature time to visit — the pavilions and surrounding paths are lit after dark, and the complex is often kept open into the evening specifically for this reason, drawing crowds especially on weekends and during spring cherry blossom season and autumn foliage season. For a quieter, less crowded experience with better photography light on the water, an early morning visit shortly after opening is a good alternative, before tour buses arrive. Spring (April) and autumn (October–November) offer the most comfortable walking weather; summer visits are humid, and the reflective night views can still be worthwhile even in the heat since the walk is short and shaded pathways line parts of the pond.

## Nearby & Tips

Donggung and Wolji sits within walking distance of several other Silla-era sites, making it easy to build a half-day historic walking loop: Cheomseongdae (one of the oldest surviving astronomical observatories in East Asia), Daereungwon/Tumuli Park with its grass-covered royal burial mounds (including Cheonmachong, which visitors can enter), and Gyeongju National Museum. Confirm current opening hours and any admission fees before visiting, as these can change seasonally — the site has historically charged a modest entrance fee. Wear shoes suited for gravel paths, and if visiting at night, note that the illuminated pavilion views are best from the western walkway looking east across the pond.
