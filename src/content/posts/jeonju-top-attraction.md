## Why Jeonju Hanok Village Is the Draw

Jeonju, the capital of North Jeolla Province, is best known nationally as the birthplace of bibimbap and as home to South Korea's largest cluster of traditional hanok housing. Jeonju Hanok Maeul packs roughly 800 hanok structures into a walkable district in Pungnam-dong and Gyo-dong, just south of the old city center. Unlike reconstructed "folk villages" elsewhere, many of these houses have been continuously inhabited since the early 20th century, and the neighborhood still functions as a living residential area alongside its tourist shops, guesthouses, cafes, and museums. The tiled roofs, narrow alleys, and mountain backdrop (Hanbyeoksan to the east) give it a distinct visual identity that's made it one of the most Instagrammed districts in Korea.

## Getting There

Jeonju doesn't have a subway system, so most visitors arrive by KTX or intercity bus. From Yongsan Station in Seoul, the KTX to Jeonju Station takes roughly 1.5–2 hours depending on the route. From Jeonju Station, the hanok village is about 15–20 minutes by taxi, or take a local city bus (routes such as 79, 79-1, and others run toward Gaeksari/Hanok Village — confirm the current route number and stop at the station's information desk, since bus numbers occasionally change). If arriving by intercity bus, Jeonju Express Bus Terminal is a similar distance away by taxi. Within the village itself, everything is walkable; cars are discouraged in the core lanes.

## What to See and Eat

Start at **Gyeonggijeon Shrine**, which houses a portrait of Yi Seong-gye, founder of the Joseon Dynasty — it's the historical anchor of the district and a good orientation point. A few minutes' walk away is **Jeondong Catholic Cathedral**, a Romanesque brick building from the early 1900s that's one of the oldest Western-style churches in Korea and a striking contrast to the surrounding hanok roofs. Many visitors rent a hanbok (traditional dress) from one of the numerous rental shops lining the main streets — wearing one often grants free or discounted entry to sites like Gyeonggijeon.

Food is central to any visit. Jeonju bibimbap, served with a wider variety of namul (seasoned vegetables) and often raw beef, is the dish to order at a sit-down restaurant. On the street-snack side, look for **kongnamul gukbap** (bean sprout soup with rice), Jeonju-style **choco pie** stalls, hard-shell **flower bread (kkotppang)**, and skewered snacks along Eunhaengno Street (the "gastro street" bordering the hanok area). The **Nambu Market**, adjacent to the village, runs a night market on weekend evenings with cheap eats and live music that's worth timing a visit around.

For a wider view, climb to **Omokdae**, a small pavilion on a hill at the edge of the village — the steps up are short but reward you with a rooftop panorama of the hanok district, especially good near sunset.

## When to Go

Spring (April) and autumn (October–November) bring the most comfortable weather and the best light for photos, but also the largest weekend crowds — expect the main lanes to be genuinely packed on Saturday afternoons. Weekday mornings before 10 a.m. are noticeably quieter. Evening visits, after most day-trippers have left, let you see the streets lit softly and the cathedral and shrine gates illuminated, plus cooler temperatures for walking in a rented hanbok. Summer is hot and humid; winter is cold but far less crowded and the tiled roofs look striking dusted with snow.

## Nearby & Practical Tips

Budget half a day minimum, a full day if you want to include a museum stop (the Jeonju Traditional Wine Museum and several small craft museums sit within the village) and a sit-down meal rather than just street food. The district connects easily on foot to Nambu Market and Pungnammun Gate, one of Jeonju's remaining old city gates, worth a five-minute detour. Confirm current museum hours and any shrine admission fees locally, as these change seasonally.
