## What to know

Jeonju is best known internationally for its large, well-preserved cluster of traditional **hanok** (Korean-style tile-roofed) houses, many of which have been converted into guesthouses, tea houses, craft workshops, small museums, and restaurants. This district is the reason most visitors add Jeonju to a Korea itinerary, and it functions less like a single "sight" and more like an entire walkable neighborhood you explore at your own pace.

Beyond the architecture itself, the area is closely tied to Jeonju's reputation as a food city — it is widely considered the birthplace of bibimbap, and the streets around the hanok district are lined with restaurants and street-food stalls serving regional specialties. Renting a hanbok (traditional Korean dress) for a few hours is a popular activity here, and you'll see many visitors, both local and international, walking the alleys in colorful outfits for photos in front of the traditional buildings.

## How to get there

Jeonju is most commonly reached by train (from Seoul's Yongsan Station) or by intercity/express bus, with journey times of roughly two to three hours depending on the service. Once in Jeonju, the historic hanok district is a short taxi ride from the train station or bus terminal — it's compact enough that once you arrive, everything is walkable, and you won't need public transit within the neighborhood itself.

If you're arriving by train, ask your accommodation or the station information desk about the most direct local bus or taxi route, since Jeonju's train station sits a bit outside the historic core.

## Tips

- **Timing matters:** The district gets very crowded with day-trippers and tour groups from mid-morning through late afternoon, especially on weekends. Arriving early in the morning or staying into the evening lets you see the traditional streets with far fewer people and better light for photos.
- **Wear comfortable shoes:** The area is built around traditional alleyways with uneven stone paths, and most of what makes it enjoyable is simply wandering rather than rushing between fixed sights.
- **Try hanbok rental:** If you want the classic experience of strolling the hanok streets in traditional dress, look for rental shops clustered near the entrance to the district — comparing a couple of options for price and included accessories is worth the extra few minutes.
- **Budget time for food:** Jeonju's culinary reputation is a major part of the visit. Leave room in your schedule to sit down for a proper meal rather than just snacking, since the city's food culture is as much a draw as the architecture.
- **Carry cash:** Smaller stalls and traditional craft shops in older neighborhoods across Korea sometimes prefer cash, so it's worth having some on hand even though card payment is widely accepted.
- **Check the weather:** Because this is fundamentally an outdoor, walking-based attraction, a rainy or extremely hot day changes the experience significantly — plan indoor backup activities like tea houses or small museums if the forecast looks poor.
