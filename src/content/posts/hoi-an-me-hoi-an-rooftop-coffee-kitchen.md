## Why go

Old Town Hoi An is famously low-rise — a UNESCO-protected grid of 15th- to 19th-century merchant houses, tube houses, and pagodas where new construction is tightly restricted. That scarcity of height is exactly what makes a spot like Mê Hội An Rooftop Coffee & Kitchen stand out: it's one of the few places inside the historic core with a genuine multi-story rooftop terrace looking straight down over the tiled roofs, the Thu Bồn River glimpses, and the yellow-painted facades the town is known for. With a 4.9 rating across nearly 2,500 reviews, it's become one of the most talked-about "view cafés" in the Ancient Town, popular with visitors chasing the golden-hour shot before heading out for the lantern-lit evening.

## Getting there

The address is 166 Trần Phú, right in the pedestrianized core of Hoi An Ancient Town. Trần Phú is one of the town's main historic streets, running roughly parallel to the river and connecting toward the Japanese Covered Bridge (Chùa Cầu) at its western end — the café is an easy 5–10 minute walk from the bridge. Hoi An's Old Town has no metro or public bus network reaching this deep into the pedestrian zone, so the practical options are on foot, by bicycle (most hotels rent them), or by taxi/Grab dropped at the nearest vehicle-access point, since parts of Trần Phú restrict cars and motorbikes during set hours (roughly mid-morning through late evening). If arriving from An Bàng or Cửa Đại beach, it's about a 15–20 minute ride by taxi or scooter. Remember that entry into several Old Town heritage streets requires the Hoi An Old Town ticket for certain monuments (not for the café itself), sold at ticket booths near the main entrances.

## What to see / eat

The draw here is vertical: multiple open-air rooftop levels stacked above a ground-floor coffee bar, giving layered vantage points over the tiled roofline of the Ancient Town — a rare sight in a town where most buildings top out at one or two stories. Go for Vietnamese coffee in its classic forms: cà phê sữa đá (iced milk coffee) or the egg-coffee-adjacent drinks that have become an Instagram staple across central Vietnam, alongside fruit teas and smoothies suited to the tropical heat. The "Kitchen" side of the menu typically runs to Hoi An and central Vietnamese staples — think banh mi, spring rolls, and rice or noodle plates — making it workable for a light lunch as well as a coffee stop. The main reason people line up, though, is photography: the rooftop framing of red-tiled roofs against the river and, later, the lantern glow at dusk, is the money shot most visitors come for.

## When to go

Late afternoon, roughly 4:30–6:30pm, is the sweet spot — you get daylight views of the Old Town roofscape, then watch the transition into the lantern-lit evening without having to change location. Sunrise (around 6–7am) is a quieter, cooler alternative with far thinner crowds. Hoi An's dry season, February through May, offers the most reliable clear skies for the rooftop view; the wetter months (September–December) bring dramatic light but a real chance of rain closing the open-air terrace early. If your visit coincides with the monthly Hoi An Lantern Festival (held on the 14th day of the lunar month, when the Old Town switches off electric lights in favor of lanterns), expect the rooftop to be significantly busier and arrive well before dusk.

## Nearby & tips

Pair the visit with the Japanese Covered Bridge, Tan Ky Ancient House, and the riverside night market, all within a 10-minute walk. Because tables at the top levels are limited and views are the whole point, plan for a wait during peak hours rather than counting on a walk-in window seat — arriving right at opening or well before sunset improves your odds considerably.
