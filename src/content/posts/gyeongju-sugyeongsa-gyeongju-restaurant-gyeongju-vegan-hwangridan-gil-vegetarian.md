## Why Sugyeongsa stands out

Gyeongju's dining scene leans heavily on grilled meats, hanjeongsik banquets, and street snacks around Hwangridan-gil, which makes a dedicated vegan/vegetarian address like Sugyeongsa (수경사) genuinely useful rather than a niche curiosity. The name itself signals its leanings toward temple-food traditions — the "sa" suffix echoes Buddhist temple naming, and the cooking style follows suit: mild seasoning, fermented pastes, tofu, wild greens (namul), and mushrooms rather than fish sauce, meat stock, or shellfish that quietly show up in a lot of "vegetarian-friendly" Korean food. A 4.9 average across 268 reviews is an unusually strong signal for a restaurant of this size, suggesting consistency rather than a one-off viral moment.

## Where it is and how to get there

The address is 4-1 Sajeong-dong, Gyeongju — a short walk from the Hwangridan-gil and Hwangnam-dong area, the historic-tomb-park district where most visitors already spend a half-day wandering past the grassy tumuli of Daereungwon and the Cheomseongdae observatory. If you're staying near Hwangridan-gil's cafes and hanbok rental shops, Sugyeongsa is reachable on foot in well under fifteen minutes; from Gyeongju's KTX station (Singyeongju) or the older Gyeongju train station, a taxi is the simplest option since local buses can be infrequent and route names aren't always posted in English. Sajeong-dong sits inland from the tumuli park, so pair a visit with a stroll through the burial mounds either right before or after eating.

## What to expect on the menu

Expect the format familiar from Korean temple-food dining: a set meal (jeongsik-style) built around rice, doenjang or miso-based soup, and a rotating spread of banchan — seasoned bracken, spinach, bean sprouts, pickled radish, braised tofu, and often a mushroom-based "meat substitute" dish like a bulgogi-style mushroom stir-fry. Dishes change with the season, since much of what defines vegan Korean cooking is whatever namul (foraged or farmed greens) is in peak condition. Portions tend to be generous relative to price for a sit-down vegetarian restaurant, and the format suits a leisurely lunch rather than a rushed bite between sightseeing stops. Because it's a small operation, menu items and daily availability can shift — it's worth going in flexible rather than fixated on one specific dish.

## Best time to visit

Lunch is the safer bet in most small Korean restaurants outside the big cities, since some close mid-afternoon before a limited dinner service or shut earlier once the day's prepared banchan runs out. Weekday visits tend to be calmer; Hwangridan-gil gets crowded with hanbok-clad day-trippers on weekends and during cherry blossom season (late March–early April) and the autumn foliage weeks (late October–November), and that spillover can affect wait times at nearby restaurants too. Given the high review count relative to size, arriving right at opening or reserving ahead — if the restaurant offers phone reservations — is a reasonable precaution, especially in peak travel months.

## Nearby and practical tips

Because Sugyeongsa sits close to Gyeongju's tomb park and Hwangridan-gil, it works well as a bookend to a walking itinerary: tumuli park in the morning, temple-food lunch here, then coffee and dessert cafes back on Hwangridan-gil in the afternoon. Vegan and vegetarian travelers in particular should treat this as a rare reliable stop, since cross-contamination and hidden animal-based stocks are common pitfalls elsewhere in the city.
