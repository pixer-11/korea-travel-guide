## Why go

Kata Beach is packed with beach-club restaurants charging resort prices for so-so pad thai, which makes a spot like Hug Street Food stand out. It's a no-frills, street-food-style eatery on Thanon Kake Kwan, the small road that runs behind Kata Beach's main shoreline strip, and it's earned an unusually strong 4.8 rating across more than 400 reviews — a sign that the cooking is consistent, not just cheap. This is the kind of place regulars return to on repeat trips to Phuket precisely because it hasn't been remodeled into a Instagram-lit tourist trap.

## Getting there

Hug Street Food sits at 1 Thanon Kake Kwan, Tambon Karon, in the Kata Beach area of Mueang Phuket district — administratively part of Karon subdistrict even though the address says "Kata Beach," since the two beaches sit right next to each other along the same coastal road. There's no rail or metro system in Phuket, so getting here means a rental scooter, a Grab ride, or a short walk if you're staying at one of the hotels or guesthouses clustered around Kata Beach Road. If you're coming from Karon Beach, it's roughly a 10–15 minute drive south along the coastal road; from Phuket Old Town it's about 40 minutes to an hour depending on traffic through Chalong. Ask your hotel to point you to Thanon Kake Kwan specifically, since Kata has a few similarly named side streets.

## What to eat

Expect the backbone of Thai street-food menus: pad thai with prawns or chicken, som tam (green papaya salad, ask for it "mai phet" if you want it not spicy), moo ping (grilled pork skewers), fried rice, basil chicken (pad kra pao), and likely a rotating curry or two. Portions at street-food stalls like this tend to be built for quick, casual eating rather than leisurely fine dining — order two or three dishes to share across a small table rather than one large plate each. Prices at this style of Kata eatery are typically a fraction of the beachfront restaurants a few hundred meters away, though exact menu prices shift year to year, so treat any number you've read online as a rough guide rather than gospel.

## When to go

Early evening — around 6 to 7:30pm — is usually the sweet spot: the day's heat has broken, ingredients are fresh from the day's prep, and you'll beat the post-sunset rush from beachgoers heading to dinner. Lunchtime can also work if you want something quick between beach sessions, though some street-food stalls in Kata close for a few hours mid-afternoon. High season (November through March) brings noticeably more foot traffic and possible short queues at popular stalls, so shoulder months like April or October can mean a quieter, faster meal without sacrificing much in weather. As with most independent street-food spots, hours can vary by season or the owner's schedule, so confirm current opening times before making it your dinner plan for the night.

## Nearby & tips

Kata Beach itself is a two-minute walk from most points on Thanon Kake Kwan, making this an easy dinner-then-sunset combination. The Kata Viewpoint (also called Karon Viewpoint), overlooking three beaches — Kata, Kata Noi, and Karon — is a short scooter ride south and pairs well with an earlier meal here. Kata also has a modest night market feel along its side streets, so budget time to wander rather than eating and leaving immediately. Bring small cash denominations, as many street-food stalls in Thailand still prefer baht over cards for quick transactions, and don't expect English menus to be elaborate — pointing at what looks good, or asking staff for their recommendation, is standard practice and usually rewarded with the best dish of the night.
