## Why go

Dedicated in 1886, the Statue of Liberty is one of the most recognized monuments on Earth and a National Monument administered by the National Park Service. Designed by French sculptor Frédéric Auguste Bartholdi with an internal iron framework by Gustave Eiffel, the copper statue was a gift from France marking the centennial of American independence and the two nations' alliance. With a 4.7 rating from over 112,000 reviews, it remains one of New York's most consistently praised attractions — not just for the statue itself but for the harbor views back toward Lower Manhattan and Brooklyn.

## Getting there

The only authorized way onto Liberty Island is via Statue City Cruises, the official ferry concessionaire. Boats depart from Battery Park at the southern tip of Manhattan (nearest subway: South Ferry on the 1 train, or Whitehall Street on the R/W line) and from Liberty State Park in Jersey City, New Jersey. Ferries run in a loop stopping first at Liberty Island, then Ellis Island, before returning — one ticket covers both. Do not book third-party "tour" boats that only circle the harbor without landing if you actually want to walk the island; read the ticket description carefully. Arrive at least 30–45 minutes before your ferry slot for airport-style security screening, which is mandatory before boarding.

## What to see and do

Once on Liberty Island, the base ticket lets you explore the grounds and the Statue of Liberty Museum, opened in 2019, which houses the original 1886 torch and exhibits on the statue's design, construction, and symbolism — a good option if you don't have pedestal or crown access, since it delivers close-up views without the extra climb. Pedestal access includes an elevator to the top of the base for close-up views of the statue's structure and skirt. Crown access is far more limited, requires a separate reservation months ahead, and involves climbing roughly 162 narrow, spiral steps with no elevator — not recommended for anyone with mobility or claustrophobia concerns.

From the island, allow time to simply walk the perimeter promenade for photos with the Manhattan skyline, Brooklyn, and the Verrazzano-Narrows Bridge in the background. Afterward, most visitors continue on the same ferry loop to Ellis Island, home to the National Museum of Immigration inside the restored Great Hall, where millions of immigrants were processed between 1892 and 1954. Budget 45 minutes to an hour there if genealogy or immigration history interests you.

## When to go

Early morning departures (the first ferry of the day) consistently have the shortest security lines and the softest light for photography, with the statue's copper-green patina glowing against the morning sky. Weekdays are noticeably quieter than weekends year-round. Spring (April–May) and fall (September–October) bring mild temperatures and clearer skies than the humid peak of summer; winter is the least crowded season but ferries can be delayed by wind or fog. Plan for a half-day minimum — roughly 4 to 5 hours round trip — if you're visiting both islands.

## Nearby & tips

Battery Park itself is worth 20–30 minutes before or after your ferry, with the SeaGlass Carousel and views toward Governors Island. Nearby in Lower Manhattan, the 9/11 Memorial & Museum and Wall Street's Charging Bull are both within a 15–20 minute walk of Battery Park, making it easy to combine into a single downtown day. Bring a light jacket even in summer — harbor winds on the ferry deck are cooler than the city streets. Since crown tickets sell out fastest, often weeks or months out, book directly through the official Statue City Cruises or National Park Service websites as soon as your travel dates are fixed, and always confirm current hours and ticket tiers before your trip, since schedules shift seasonally.
