## Why go

Mermaid Coffee Roasters has quietly become one of Sapporo's most talked-about coffee addresses, and the numbers back it up: a 4.8-star average across more than 1,500 reviews is rare for any cafe, let alone one tucked into a mid-sized city block in Chuo Ward. This isn't a mass-market chain — it's a roaster-led operation where the beans are sourced, roasted, and brewed with the kind of obsessive attention that specialty coffee fans travel for. If you care about where your cup comes from, or you just want to see what a genuinely trendy, design-conscious Japanese cafe looks like away from Tokyo, this is a worthwhile detour.

## Getting there

The address is 1-chōme-12-7 Minami 3 Jōnishi, Chuo Ward, Sapporo — right in the Minami 3/Susukino grid, one of central Sapporo's densest dining and nightlife pockets. The easiest approach is the Namboku Line to Susukino Station, then a short walk north through the Minami 3-jo Nishi blocks; the area is also walkable from Odori Station (about 10–12 minutes) if you're coming from the Odori Park side of downtown. Because the neighborhood is a grid of small numbered blocks, it's worth pulling up the exact address on a map app before you set out rather than relying on street signage alone — many storefronts here are unmarked from the street level, cafe-scene style. If you're staying near Sapporo Station, the whole trip by subway plus walking runs about 15–20 minutes door to door.

## What to see and drink

This is a roastery-cafe, so the point is the coffee itself: expect single-origin pour-overs, a rotating selection of roasts, and espresso-based drinks made with beans roasted on-site or nearby. Specialty roasters in Japan typically display their current bean lineup on a chalkboard or card at the counter, with tasting notes (fruit, floral, chocolate, nutty) — take a minute to read through before ordering, and don't hesitate to ask staff for a recommendation based on whether you prefer bright/acidic or heavier, darker profiles. Seating tends to be limited and design-forward — expect concrete, wood, and pared-back interiors typical of Japan's third-wave coffee aesthetic — so this is a sit-and-savor stop rather than a rushed one. Pastries or light bites, if offered, usually rotate seasonally and sell out earlier in the day, so don't count on a full food menu being available late afternoon.

## When to go

Weekday mornings (opening through around 11am) are the best window for a seat without a wait, given the cafe's popularity and Susukino's general foot traffic picking up from midday onward. Weekends and early evenings can mean a queue, especially since Susukino is a nightlife district that stays lively into the evening — the contrast of a quiet coffee ritual before the area's bars and restaurants wake up is part of the appeal. Sapporo winters (December–March) are genuinely cold, so a coffee stop here pairs naturally with a warm indoor break between visits to the Sapporo TV Tower or Odori Park's Snow Festival grounds nearby. Hours and any seasonal closures should be checked on the day of your visit, since small independent roasters often adjust schedules around holidays.

## Nearby & tips

Susukino is Sapporo's largest entertainment district, so combine your coffee stop with a wander through its ramen alleys and izakaya streets — Ramen Yokocho, the famous cluster of tiny ramen shops, is within easy walking distance. Odori Park, the Sapporo TV Tower, and the Susukino subway station's shopping arcades are all close enough to fold into a half-day loop with the cafe as either your starting point or a mid-afternoon reset. Budget 30–45 minutes for a proper cup rather than a grab-and-go visit — part of the value here is the pacing. If you're a coffee enthusiast, ask whether beans are available for purchase to take home; roasters with this level of reputation often sell retail bags, which make for a far more distinctive souvenir than typical Hokkaido snack boxes.
