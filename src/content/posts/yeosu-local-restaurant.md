## What to know

Yeosu is a port city on Korea's south coast, and its food identity is built almost entirely around fresh seafood pulled from the surrounding waters. Rather than one iconic "must-visit" restaurant, the city is known for a food culture: rows of family-run seafood houses, raw fish (hoe) halls, and grilled or braised fish specialists clustered around the harbor, fish markets, and ferry terminal areas. If you're searching for "the best local restaurant in Yeosu," it's more useful to think in terms of neighborhoods and dish types than a single address, since the best places tend to be small, independently run, and can change or expand from year to year.

Common regional specialties travelers ask about include raw seafood platters (hoe), braised or grilled fish such as hairtail or conger eel, and mixed seafood hot pots. Menus in these neighborhood spots are often seasonal, depending on the day's catch, so don't be surprised if what's available changes with the weather and season.

## How to get there

Yeosu is served by the KTX (Yeosu Expo Station) and by long-distance buses into Yeosu Bus Terminal, both of which connect fairly easily to the harbor and downtown dining areas by taxi or local bus. The Yeosu Passenger Terminal, near the old town and the famous Yeosu night sea views, is a good general orientation point — many local eateries are within walking distance of it. If you're staying near Dolsan Bridge or the Expo area, those neighborhoods also have their own concentrations of seafood restaurants, generally geared toward both locals and tourists enjoying the waterfront at night.

As with most Korean cities, taxis are affordable and easy to flag for short hops between the terminal, harbor, and market areas, which is often the simplest way to reach a specific restaurant cluster if you don't want to navigate local bus routes.

## Tips

- **Follow the locals, not the signage.** In port cities like Yeosu, a restaurant packed with local diners at odd hours (not just tourist rush times) is usually a strong sign of quality and freshness.
- **Ask what's in season.** Since much of the menu depends on the day's catch, asking staff what's fresh or recommended that day often gets better results than ordering from a fixed idea of what you want.
- **Carry cash.** Many small, family-run seafood restaurants in Korea's smaller port cities still prefer cash or have card minimums, so it's worth having won on hand.
- **Go where the view is.** Some of Yeosu's most memorable meals are less about a famous kitchen and more about pairing solid seafood with the harbor or night-sea scenery the city is known for — factor that into where you choose to sit down.
- **Double-check current details before you go.** Restaurant hours, prices, and even names can shift; confirming with your accommodation or a current map app close to your visit date is a good habit in this region.
