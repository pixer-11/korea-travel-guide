## Why Yeosu's local food scene is worth the detour

Yeosu sits on Korea's southern coast where the Namhae Sea's tidal flats and deep fishing grounds meet, giving the city an outsized reputation for raw and fermented seafood that Seoulites travel hours to eat. Unlike Busan's razzle-dazzle Jagalchi Market, Yeosu's food culture is quieter and more residential — small, family-run hapjib (set-meal restaurants) and market stalls that have served the same handful of dishes for decades. The three dishes locals point to first are ganjang gejang (blue crab marinated in soy sauce, eaten raw), dolgae-jang (a spicy blood-clam and vegetable dish, often mixed with rice and toasted seaweed), and jangeo gui (grilled conger eel, a summer specialty tied to Yeosu's reputation as Korea's eel capital). Expect banchan (side dishes) built around gat-kimchi, made from the mustard leaf grown specifically in Yeosu's Dolsan district — a hyperlocal ingredient you won't taste the same way anywhere else.

## Getting there

Yeosu-Expo Station is the terminus for KTX trains from Seoul (roughly 3 hours) and is the natural base for exploring on foot or by local bus. From the station, most of the harborside eating areas — Jungang-dong, near Yeosu Passenger Terminal, and the Yeosu Enodeul Night Market — are a 10–15 minute taxi ride or a longer walk. If you're coming from Yeosu Airport, it's about a 40-minute drive into the city center. Local city buses (numbers vary by route, check signage at stops) connect the KTX station to Odongdo Island and the Dolsan Bridge area, both of which have concentrations of seafood restaurants overlooking the water.

## What to eat and where to look

Seek out Seodong Market and Gyodong Market, two adjacent traditional markets near central Yeosu that operate mostly in the morning and early afternoon — this is where locals buy the day's catch and where several stalls serve prepared meals using it. For ganjang gejang, look for restaurants clustered around the harbor in Jungang-dong that display live crabs in tanks out front; portions are typically ordered as a set (jeongsik) with rice, soup, and 8–10 banchan. Jangeo gui is best sought on Dolsan Island, across the Dolsan Bridge, where a strip of eel-specialty restaurants has operated for generations, some grilling the eel tableside over charcoal. If you want a lighter, cheaper option, look for haemul kalguksu (seafood noodle soup) or bibim naengmyeon at smaller diners — a bowl rarely runs past the mid-teens in thousands of won, though prices should always be confirmed on-site since they shift year to year.

## When to go and how long to stay

Morning through early afternoon (roughly 8am–2pm) is when markets are liveliest and seafood is freshest, since much of it arrives with the morning boats. Evenings are better spent at the harborside restaurants near Yeosu Passenger Terminal or the Yeosu Night Sea Train area, where dinner pairs naturally with the city's famous night views along the waterfront. Budget 1.5–2 hours for a proper seafood meal, especially if raw crab or a grilled set is involved — these are meant to be eaten slowly with multiple banchan refills. Autumn and winter are peak season for crab, while summer is prime for eel, so the "best" dish shifts with the calendar.

## Nearby & practical tips

Pair a meal with a walk around Odongdo Island (connected to the mainland by a short causeway, famous for its camellia trees and lighthouse) or a ride on the Yeosu Marine Cable Car, which links Jasan Park to Dolsan Park with sea views in both directions — both are within a short taxi ride of the main eating districts. Cash is still useful at smaller market stalls, though card acceptance has become standard at most sit-down restaurants. If ordering ganjang gejang for the first time, ask staff how to eat it (cracking the shell and using the marinade as a dipping sauce for rice) — presentation varies slightly by restaurant, and the etiquette isn't always obvious to first-timers.
