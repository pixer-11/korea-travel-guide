## Why Hahoe Village Is Worth the Detour

Andong sits off the main Seoul–Busan tourist corridor, which is exactly why Hahoe Folk Village has stayed genuine rather than becoming a reconstructed theme park. Real families — many from the Ryu clan of Pungsan, who founded the settlement in the 14th–15th century — still live in the tile-roofed and thatched-roof hanok that line the village's curving lanes. The village takes its name from the way the Nakdong River wraps around it in a near-360-degree loop ("hahoe" means "river turns"), a layout that traditional Korean geomancy considered auspicious. UNESCO recognized Hahoe, together with the nearby village of Yangdong, as a World Heritage Site in 2010 for this exceptionally well-preserved clan-village landscape.

## Getting There

Hahoe Village sits at 186 Jeonseo-ro, Pungcheon-myeon, about 24 km southwest of central Andong. There's no train station in the village itself — travelers typically reach Andong first, either by intercity bus from Seoul's Dong-Seoul Terminal (roughly 3 hours) or by train to Andong Station on the Yeongdong/Gyeongbuk line network. From Andong's bus terminal or station area, city bus 46 runs directly to Hahoe Village several times a day; a taxi takes about 30-40 minutes. Many visitors also base themselves in Andong and book a half-day taxi or join a local shuttle tour that pairs Hahoe with the Andong Folk Museum. Because bus frequency is limited, check the current timetable before setting out, and build in flexibility if you're relying on public transit alone.

## What to See and Do Inside the Village

Once inside, the village itself charges a small entry fee and requires visitors to leave cars at the entrance, walking or taking a shuttle cart the rest of the way. Wander the grid of packed-earth lanes past Yangjindang and Chunghyodang, two of the grandest tile-roofed residences, both centuries old and still maintained by descendants. The thatched commoners' houses closer to the river give a rawer sense of pre-modern rural life. Don't skip the Hahoe Mask Museum near the entrance, which displays the wooden masks used in the Hahoe Byeolsingut Talnori, a satirical shamanistic mask dance historically performed to appease village spirits and lampoon the yangban aristocracy; if you're visiting on a weekend, check whether a live performance is scheduled at the on-site theater. Across the river, the pine grove of Manson Jeongnim and the sheer cliff of Buyongdae offer the village's signature view — reachable either by a small ferry crossing near the village or via a walking path around the bend. Climbing or ferrying over to Buyongdae for the panoramic shot of the river's curve and the rooftops below is the single most memorable thing to do here, and it's easy to underestimate how much time it adds, so budget accordingly.

## When to Go and How Long to Stay

Spring (April-May) and autumn (October-November) bring the best light and comfortable walking temperatures, plus the surrounding hills turn color in fall. Late September or early October occasionally overlaps with the Andong International Mask Dance Festival, held in Andong city, which pairs naturally with a Hahoe visit. Summers are humid and the riverside can be busy with domestic tour groups; winters are quiet but stark, with bare trees and a slower pace. Most visitors spend 3-4 hours exploring the lanes, museum, and riverside, longer if crossing to Buyongdae or catching a mask dance performance.

## Nearby and Practical Tips

Combine Hahoe with the separate Andong Mask Museum in the city center for context before or after your visit, and consider Dosan Seowon, a Confucian academy about 30 minutes northeast, if you have a full day for Andong's Joseon-era heritage. Local specialties worth trying nearby include Andong jjimdak (braised chicken) and heotgae-jip tofu dishes tied to the region's traditional Confucian ceremonial food. Wear shoes suited to dirt paths and uneven stone, and note that some historic houses are private residences — stick to marked walkways and be respectful when passing occupied homes.
