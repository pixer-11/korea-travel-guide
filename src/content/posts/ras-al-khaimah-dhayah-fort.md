## Why go

Dhayah Fort is the last hill fort standing in the UAE — most of the country's historic forts sit at ground level, but this one crowns a natural rocky outcrop above the palm groves of Ar Rams. Its mud-brick and stone towers date to the 19th century, and the fort holds a genuinely dramatic place in Emirati history: it's where the ruling Qawasim family made their final stand against a British East India Company assault in 1819, an event that led directly to the treaties that shaped the modern "Trucial States." Unlike restored showpiece forts elsewhere in the Gulf, Dhayah still feels raw and exposed on its hilltop, which is exactly what makes the view from the top worth the climb.

## Getting there

Dhayah Fort is roughly 30-40 km north of Ras Al Khaimah city, near the town of Ar Rams, on the road toward the Musandam border. There's no metro or public rail in RAK, so the practical options are a rental car, taxi, or organized tour — a car is easiest since the site sits just off the main coastal highway with a small parking area at the base of the hill. Most visitors combine it with a loop up the northern RAK coast, pairing it with nearby Al Rams's fishing harbor or the drive toward Musandam and Jebel Jais. Signage at the base marks the start of the path up.

## What to see

The approach is a stone staircase cut into the hillside — a few hundred steps, steep in places, leading to the fort's remaining tower and wall fragments at the summit. There's no grand palace interior to tour; the appeal is architectural and geographic rather than curatorial. At the top, the payoff is a 360-degree view: the turquoise lagoons and mangrove flats of RAK's coastline on one side, date palm plantations and the Hajar Mountains on the other. The fort's mud-and-stone construction, eroded by weather and time, gives a strong sense of how defensible this position was — it's easy to see why it held out during the 1819 siege before the Qawasim ultimately surrendered. Bring water; there's little shade once you start climbing.

## When to go

Late afternoon, an hour or two before sunset, is the best window — the heat eases, the staircase is in partial shade, and the light over the mountains and lagoon turns golden. Summer (June to September) midday visits are genuinely uncomfortable given the exposed stone steps and lack of shade; if visiting between May and September, aim for early morning or just before sunset instead. Winter (November to March) is the most pleasant season overall for this part of RAK, with cooler temperatures making the climb easy at almost any hour.

## How to visit like a local

Dhayah Fort is well-known enough to draw a steady stream of visitors and tour groups, but it's not overwhelmed the way some Gulf heritage sites are — arriving on a weekday morning or that late-afternoon window keeps it quiet even in peak season. There's typically no ticket booth or barrier at the base, so there's nothing to book in advance and no queue to navigate; just park, walk up, and come back down at your own pace. Wear closed shoes with grip — sandals are a common mistake on the uneven stone steps, and the climb is more athletic than it looks in photos. There's rarely shade or a place to sit at the top, so most people spend 15-20 minutes at the summit taking in the view before heading back down. Since this is an open historical site rather than a commercial attraction, there's no tipping expected and no vendors at the top — bring your own water rather than expecting to buy any on site. Treat the fort itself gently: it's an unrestored, weathered structure, so avoid climbing on crumbling wall sections for photos.

## Nearby stops

Combine the visit with the wider northern RAK coastline: Al Rams' small fishing harbor is a few minutes away, and the road continues toward Sha'am and the Musandam Peninsula for those continuing into Oman. Jebel Jais, the UAE's highest peak with its zipline and mountain roads, is a worthwhile detour on the same day if starting early enough.
