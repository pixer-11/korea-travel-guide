## Why go

teamLab Planets TOKYO DMM isn't a museum you view from a distance — you walk through it, often ankle-deep in water, surrounded by projected light, mirrors, and fog. Rated 4.5 stars across more than 54,000 reviews, it's become one of Tokyo's defining "trendy" attractions, less a gallery of static works than an immersive, slightly disorienting sensory experience built around themes of nature, impermanence, and the blurred boundary between body and artwork. It's the kind of place that photographs spectacularly but is genuinely more affecting in person — the koi-and-flower projection room and the mirrored infinity spaces have a scale and softness that don't fully translate to a phone screen.

## Getting there

The museum sits at 6-chōme-1-16 Toyosu, Koto City, in the Toyosu waterfront district of Tokyo Bay, an area also known for Toyosu Market and its sushi breakfast spots. The easiest access point is Toyosu Station, served by both the Yurikamome (automated waterfront line) and the Tokyo Metro Yurakucho Line — from the station it's roughly a 5-10 minute walk, and signage in the area generally points visitors toward the entrance. Toyosu is a reclaimed island neighborhood, so expect wide streets, modern office towers, and a noticeably different feel from central Tokyo's older districts. Taxis from Ginza or Tsukiji take about 15-20 minutes depending on traffic.

## What to expect inside

teamLab Planets is split into a handful of large-scale installations, most famously the water areas — visitors walk barefoot through shallow, warm water while koi-like light projections swim around their legs, and through a Botanical Garden section filled with hanging orchids intermixed with light spheres. There's also a mirror labyrinth ball room where suspended spheres drift and change color, and a "floating flower garden" of real orchids suspended overhead that seems to descend as you move slower and rise as you move faster — deliberately designed to reward lingering. Because of the water sections, the museum requires bare feet for parts of the route; lockers are provided for shoes and bags, and it's worth wearing clothes you can roll up past the knee. Skirts and dresses are discouraged for practical reasons. Expect to spend 90 minutes to two hours moving through at a relaxed pace — rushing defeats the purpose.

## When to go and how to book

Tickets are date- and time-slot specific and routinely sell out days or even weeks ahead, especially around weekends, Japanese holidays, and cherry blossom or summer travel seasons — book online in advance through the official teamLab Planets site rather than assuming walk-up entry will be possible. Weekday mornings, particularly the first entry slot, tend to have thinner crowds and give the mirror rooms a chance to feel genuinely quiet rather than jostled. Exact ticket prices and operating hours shift periodically (the museum has also announced a planned relocation/renewal in the past, so it's worth confirming the current site and hours before finalizing plans), so treat any price you see elsewhere as approximate until checked on the official page.

## Nearby & tips

Pair the visit with Toyosu Market, a short taxi or bus ride away, for an early sushi breakfast before your museum slot — many visitors do market first, museum mid-morning. Bring a small towel or use the ones sometimes provided near the water exit areas to dry your feet before re-socking. Leave extra time for the gift shop, which stocks limited-edition prints and light-based merchandise unique to the DMM location. Note that this is a genuinely popular attraction rather than a hidden gem — go in expecting other visitors in every frame, and aim to enjoy the shared, communal nature of moving through the water rooms rather than fighting for an empty shot.
