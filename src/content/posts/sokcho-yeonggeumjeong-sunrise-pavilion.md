## Why go

Yeonggeumjeong is one of those spots that photographs better than it sounds on paper: a compact, ornately painted hanok pavilion sitting on a dark volcanic rock outcrop, connected to the mainland by a short arched bridge. It's not a temple or a historic relic — the current structure is a modern rebuild — but its position, jutting into the East Sea with waves breaking directly beneath it, gives it a drama that draws in both sunrise chasers and evening strollers. At 4.3 stars across nearly 12,000 reviews, it's clearly become one of Sokcho's most-visited free attractions, yet it still feels smaller-scale and less packaged than the city's beaches or the Seorak cable car crowds.

## Getting there

The pavilion sits at 43 Yeonggeumjeong-ro in Dongmyeong-dong, on Sokcho's northeastern coastal edge, close to where the Sokcho fishing harbor meets the open sea. There's no subway in Sokcho — the nearest major transit hub is the Sokcho Express Bus Terminal or Sokcho Intercity Bus Terminal, from which it's roughly a 10-15 minute taxi ride. Local city buses also run along the coastal road toward Dongmyeong-dong; ask your accommodation for the current route number, as Sokcho's bus numbering has shifted in recent years. If you're driving or renting a car (common for this trip, since many visitors combine Sokcho with Seoraksan National Park), there's a small parking area near the site, though it fills up fast on summer weekends and holidays. Many travelers simply fold Yeonggeumjeong into a walk from Sokcho Beach or the Abai Village ferry crossing, both a short drive or a longer coastal walk away.

## What to see and do

The pavilion itself is the main event: a two-tiered structure with upturned eaves and painted dancheong brackets, sitting atop jagged black rock that's been carved by surf for centuries. Cross the short footbridge — this is the part that lights up at night, making it a popular evening photo stop even for people who skip the sunrise crowd. Once inside, there's little to "do" beyond taking in the 270-degree sea view, but that's the point: it's a lookout, not a museum. Below and around the rock, tide pools and breakwater rocks are worth a slow walk at low tide. Just south along the coast, the working harbor gives a look at Sokcho's fishing fleet and the raw-seafood stalls that supply the city's famous seafood restaurants — a natural next stop if you're hungry after sunrise.

## When to go

Sunrise is the signature experience — Sokcho's east-facing coastline means the pavilion gets unobstructed first light over open water, and locals and photographers gather on the rocks and bridge well before the sun clears the horizon. Arrive at least 20-30 minutes early to find a spot, especially in summer when sunrise comes early (around 5 a.m.) but crowds are heaviest. Alternatively, come after dusk: the bridge and pavilion illumination create a very different, moodier scene with far fewer people. Midday visits are fine for a quick look but lose the light drama that makes this place worth the detour. The site has no gate or ticket booth and is generally accessible around the clock, though always check current local notices for any storm-related closures, since the rocks and bridge are exposed to open sea.

## Nearby & tips

Combine the visit with Abai Village (reachable by a hand-pulled ferry across the channel) and the Sokcho Jungang Market for breakfast dishes like ojingeo sundae (squid sausage) or fresh sashimi. Wind off the sea can be sharp even in warmer months, so bring a light jacket regardless of season. The rocks near the pavilion are slick when wet — sturdy shoes matter more than usual here. There's no admission fee currently charged, but as with any free public site, hours and access can change without much notice, so a quick check with your accommodation or a local tourism office the day before is worth the five minutes it takes.
