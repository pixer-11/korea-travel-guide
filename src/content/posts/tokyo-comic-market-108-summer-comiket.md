## What Comiket Actually Is

Comic Market — universally shortened to "Comiket" — is not a publisher-run trade show like a Western comic con. It's a self-published fan work market: tens of thousands of individual "circles" (creators, often working solo or in small teams) rent tables to sell doujinshi, fan art books, self-produced music, and original comics. There's no film-studio booth, no big-name autograph line in the American sense. The draw is browsing an entire convention center of independent creators, many of whom only sell work twice a year, at this event and its winter counterpart. Comic Market 108 runs August 15–16, 2026, entirely at Tokyo Big Sight.

## Getting There

Tokyo Big Sight sits on the reclaimed Odaiba/Ariake waterfront in Koto City. The most reliable approach is the Yurikamome automated line to Kokusai-Tenjijo-Seimon Station, which puts you right at the venue's main entrance — this is the route most Comiket veterans use because it avoids the worst crowd bottlenecks. An alternative is the Rinkai Line to Kokusai-Tenjijo Station, a roughly 5–7 minute walk to the halls. Both stations get severely congested during peak arrival hours (from around 7–9am), so build in extra time and expect station staff actively managing queues. Taxis and driving are discouraged; there's no practical parking solution for an event this size.

## What to See and Do

Expect the show floor split across Tokyo Big Sight's East and West exhibition halls, with genre and fandom areas organized into blocks so buyers can find, say, a specific anime's fanworks section without wandering the whole building. Popular circles (often signaled by long lines forming before doors open) sell out of printed material within the first hour or two, so if there's a specific creator you want, arrive early and know their table/space number in advance from the online catalog. Outside the halls, the plaza areas and surrounding walkways are Comiket's other unofficial main event: cosplay. Hundreds of cosplayers gather in designated outdoor photo areas (weather permitting), and this people-watching is free and doesn't require an event pass. Corporate/publisher booths (a smaller adjacent section in recent Comikets) sell official merchandise and can have their own lines separate from the doujinshi circles.

## When to Go and How Long to Stay

August in Tokyo means heat and humidity in the low-to-mid 30s°C, and much of the wait to get inside happens outdoors in direct sun — this is the single biggest practical hazard of Summer Comiket, more than any crowd-navigation issue. Day one and day two typically feature different genre groupings, so serious doujinshi buyers often attend both days rather than picking one. A first-time visitor who mainly wants to experience the atmosphere and browse can reasonably do it in 3–4 hours; someone hunting specific circles across both exhibition halls should plan for a full day each day.

## Nearby & Practical Tips

Odaiba has DiverCity Tokyo Plaza and the Fuji TV Building nearby for a break from the halls, and the Toyosu fish market area is a short trip away if you want food that isn't convention-hall convenience-store fare. Bring cash in small bills — many circles are cash-only for doujinshi purchases — and a large tote or backpack, since a single day's haul of books adds up fast. Because Comiket sets its own entry system (staggered by wristband, ticket, or catalog purchase depending on the year) and because venue halls in use can shift between events, do not rely on past-year logistics: check the official Comiket website and the Tokyo Big Sight site directly in the weeks before August 15–16, 2026 for confirmed hours, any ticketing/reservation requirements, and which halls are open each day.
