## Why go

Mud Square, on Daecheon Beach in Boryeong, is ground zero for one of Korea's most famous outdoor events, the Boryeong Mud Festival. What started as a marketing push by local cosmetics companies to promote Boryeong's mineral-rich mud has grown into a full beach carnival, drawing well over a million visitors during its two-week run. Even outside festival dates, Mud Square remains a beach plaza with mud pools, photo installations, and open sand where visitors can experience the region's mud tradition on a smaller scale. With a 4.3 rating from over 900 reviews, it's clearly earned its reputation as a genuinely fun, slightly chaotic, get-dirty-on-purpose attraction rather than a polished tourist trap.

## Getting there

Mud Square sits at 5 Haesuyokjang 10-gil, right on Daecheon Beach in Boryeong-si, Chungcheongnam-do — about a 2-hour bus or train ride southwest of Seoul. The most common route is a direct KTX or intercity train to Daecheon Station, followed by a short taxi or local bus (roughly 10-15 minutes) straight to the beachfront. During festival season, Boryeong runs shuttle buses from the train station and from Seoul's major bus terminals directly to the festival grounds, which drop visitors within walking distance of Mud Square. If driving, there's beachside parking, though it fills quickly on festival weekends, so arriving before mid-morning is the practical move.

## What to see and do

The heart of Mud Square is exactly what the name promises: pools, tubs, and inflatable structures filled with Boryeong's soft grey mud, which is mined from tidal flats prized for their high mineral and germanium content. Expect mud slides, a mud prison photo-op, wrestling pits, and — during the festival — a mud king contest and color-run-style mud marches down the beach. Vendors sell mud in tubes and packets to take home, since much of it is processed into the same cosmetic lines Boryeong is known for regionally.

Because the beach is right there, the natural rhythm is: mud up, play for an hour or two, then walk straight into the Yellow Sea to rinse off before the mud dries and cakes. Outdoor shower stations near the square handle the final rinse. Budget a half-day (3-4 hours) to get the full experience without rushing — much less if you're just curious and want a quick dip, longer if you're there for festival programming like concerts and fireworks, which typically run in the evening after the daytime mud activities wind down.

## When to go

The festival itself runs for about two weeks in mid-to-late July, timed to Korea's summer school break and the start of monsoon-adjacent beach season — check the current year's official dates before booking, as they shift slightly year to year. This is peak crowds, peak energy, and peak heat, so mornings are cooler and less packed than afternoons. Outside the festival window, Mud Square and the surrounding beach are far quieter, and some mud activities may be scaled back or unavailable, so travelers specifically chasing the "mud festival" experience should target festival dates.

## Nearby & tips

Daecheon Beach itself is a long, walkable stretch with plenty of seafood restaurants and motels clustered nearby, making an overnight stay easy if arriving from Seoul. Wear clothes and footwear you don't mind staining permanently — dark colors are smarter than white — and bring a waterproof bag for phones and valuables, since mud and saltwater are equally unforgiving. Lockers and changing facilities are typically available near the beach entrance, but they get busy, so packing a spare set of clothes and flip-flops for the trip home is a smart backup. Sunscreen matters more than people expect here, since hours are spent on open sand with mud reflecting extra glare.
