## Why go

Smith & Wollensky is a New York steakhouse institution, originally opened on Manhattan's Third Avenue in 1977, and its arrival in Tokyo marks the brand's debut in Japan. The Ginza location doesn't just import the name — it brings the core identity: USDA Prime dry-aged beef, a clubby steakhouse atmosphere, and a wine program built around more than 2,000 sommelier-selected labels. For visitors who want an American-style steakhouse experience executed at a serious level, rather than a Japanese take on Western beef culture, this is currently one of the most talked-about openings in the city. The three-floor layout suggests distinct spaces — likely a bar/lounge level, a main dining room, and private or upper-floor seating — which is typical of the brand's flagship locations abroad, though exact floor-by-floor uses are worth confirming when booking.

## Getting there

The restaurant sits in Ginza, Tokyo's premier upscale shopping and dining district, which makes it an easy add-on to a day spent browsing the Chuo-dori flagship stores or visiting Kabukiza Theatre nearby. Ginza is served by multiple subway lines — the Ginza Line, Marunouchi Line, and Hibiya Line all converge at Ginza Station, while the Yurakucho Line stops at Ginza-Itchome and Yurakucho Station (JR Yamanote and Keihin-Tohoku lines) sits just a short walk from the district's edge. Given Ginza's density of similarly named cross-streets, it's worth pulling up the exact address in a map app before leaving your hotel, and confirming the nearest exit at the time of booking since Ginza Station alone has numerous numbered exits feeding directly into different store basements.

Details on the specific menu, à la carte pricing, and prix-fixe options have not been officially confirmed for this listing, so treat any third-party menu previews with caution until you check the restaurant's own channels. What is established is the concept: dry-aged USDA Prime steaks — the cut and aging process the original Manhattan location built its reputation on — served in a formal steakhouse setting, paired with an unusually deep wine list for Tokyo. ## When to go

Book dinner reservations well in advance, especially for weekend evenings — new high-profile openings in Ginza routinely fill up for weeks after launch, and a three-floor restaurant with a wine-cellar concept is designed for a leisurely two-hour-plus meal rather than a quick bite. If the location offers a lunch service, that may be a comparatively easier reservation and a lower-commitment way to sample the space before returning for a full dinner with the wine list. Because opening hours, days closed, and reservation policy can shift in the weeks after a launch, always verify directly through the restaurant's official website or a reservation platform immediately before your visit.

## Nearby & tips

Pair your visit with a pre-dinner stroll down Chuo-dori or a visit to the Ginza Six shopping complex, both within easy walking distance. If you're steakhouse-hopping or simply curious how the New York original compares, it's worth noting that Smith & Wollensky's signature move worldwide is the dry-aging program — ask your server about the current aging period on the beef being served, as steakhouses of this caliber often rotate cuts. Dress code at flagship international locations tends to be smart casual to formal, so it's sensible to lean dressier for Ginza, a neighborhood where upscale attire is the norm.
