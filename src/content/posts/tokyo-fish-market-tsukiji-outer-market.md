## Why go

Tsukiji Outer Market is the street-food and specialty-shop half of what was once Tokyo's central wholesale fish market. When the wholesale auction and main trading floor relocated to Toyosu in October 2018, the outer market — the tangle of narrow lanes lined with seafood stalls, knife shops, dried-bonito sellers, and tiny counter restaurants — stayed put in Chuo City's Tsukiji neighborhood. It's now less about wholesale commerce and more about eating your way through Tokyo's seafood culture in a few concentrated blocks, which is exactly why it draws crowds daily.

## Getting there

The market sits between Tsukiji Station (Hibiya Line, exit 1 or 2) and Shintomicho Station (Yurakucho Line), both about a 3-5 minute walk. If arriving from central Tokyo, Tsukiji Station is the more direct option. Ginza is a 15-minute walk south, so many visitors combine a morning market visit with an afternoon in Ginza's department stores. Look for the covered Namiyoke Inari Shrine area as a landmark near the market's eastern edge — it's a good orientation point if the lanes feel maze-like.

## What to eat

The market is arranged in a rough grid of stalls (locally called Jonai-gai), and the specialties worth seeking out include:

- **Fresh sashimi and kaisen-don (seafood rice bowls)** — small counters sell single skewers of tuna, uni, or scallop alongside full bowls; portions are designed for grazing rather than one big sit-down meal.
- **Tamagoyaki (rolled omelet)** — several stalls specialize in this alone, often served on a stick for easy eating while walking.
- **Grilled unagi and anago (eel)** — smoky, char-grilled right at the stall front.
- **Uni (sea urchin)** served straight from the shell, and scallops grilled to order.
- **Dashi and dried bonito (katsuobushi) shops** — not edible on the spot, but worth browsing for take-home souvenirs; several shops let you sample shavings.
- **Sushi counters** — a handful of long-standing sit-down sushi shops inside and around the market offer set breakfasts; expect a line, especially on weekends, and cash-only policies at many.

Portions at stalls are intentionally small and inexpensive so you can sample five or six different things rather than filling up at one stop. Eating while walking is normal and expected here, unlike most of Tokyo.

## When to go

Arrive early — ideally by 8am — for the freshest selection and shorter lines; by 11am, lanes narrow with crowds and some stalls sell out of popular items. The market is busiest on weekends, so weekday mornings offer a calmer pace. Most stalls and shops close by early-to-mid afternoon and many are shut on Sundays and irregular holidays, so it's not a dinner destination. If you specifically want to see the tuna auction that made Tsukiji famous, note that this now happens at Toyosu Market, a short taxi or train ride away via the Yurikamome line to Shijo-mae Station — the outer market itself no longer hosts it.

## Nearby & tips

- Bring small cash; many stalls don't take cards and ATMs are not abundant inside the market lanes.
- Wear shoes you don't mind getting slightly wet — some lanes are damp from ice and rinsing.
- Combine the visit with nearby Hamarikyu Gardens, a 10-15 minute walk south, for a quiet contrast after the market's energy.
- The market carries a strong 4.2 rating across tens of thousands of reviews, reflecting its enduring popularity despite the 2018 auction relocation — it's still considered one of Tokyo's essential food experiences, just recalibrated as a market-to-eat destination rather than a wholesale trading floor.
- Expect no formal reservations for stall food; sit-down sushi shops may allow booking, but many operate first-come, first-served with posted queue times.
