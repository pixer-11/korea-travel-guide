## Why go

Bingin has long been Uluwatu's cliffside surf enclave, better known for warungs, cliff-hugging losmen, and the famous Bingin Beach staircase than for polished dining. Pak Gula changes that equation. It comes from the team behind The Cashew Tree, one of Canggu and Uluwatu's more reliable names in the casual-cool dining scene, and it brings that same shareable, social, design-forward approach to Bingin. The restaurant is spread across two storeys, which in practice means different moods on different levels — expect a more low-key ground floor for walk-ins and a livelier upper level suited to groups and evening drinks. For visitors who've already done the beach clubs of Uluwatu and Padang Padang, Pak Gula is the kind of stop that gives the area a genuine after-dark identity rather than just a scenic day-trip stopover.

## What to expect on the menu

The signature draw is the char siu pork bao buns — a fusion dish that leans into modern pan-Asian small-plates dining rather than traditional Indonesian fare. Expect the format to follow the shareable-plates model that's become the norm at trendy Bali restaurants: order several dishes for the table, mix in a cocktail or two, and graze over a couple of hours rather than committing to one main course. The cocktail program is called out specifically as a highlight, so it's worth treating drinks as a reason to visit in their own right, not just an add-on. Because this is a newly opened venue, treat any menu details beyond the bao buns as fluid — dishes, pricing, and even format details can change quickly in the first months of operation, so don't go in expecting a fixed, unchanging lineup.

## Getting there

Pak Gula sits in the heart of Bingin, on the Bukit Peninsula south of Kuta and Seminyak, in the same general pocket as Bingin Beach's cliffside staircase and the cluster of surf warungs and villas that define the area. There's no train or public transit system on this part of Bali — the standard approach is by scooter or private driver from wherever you're staying on the Bukit or further north in Canggu/Seminyak (expect at least 45–75 minutes from Seminyak depending on traffic). Parking in Bingin is informal and can get tight during peak sunset hours, so factor in a short walk from wherever you leave a car or scooter. If you're staying in Uluwatu proper or Padang Padang, it's a quick ride of 10–20 minutes.

## When to go

Bingin's whole rhythm is built around sunset, and a two-storey restaurant with a cocktail focus is clearly designed to capture that golden-hour-into-evening window. Arriving in the late afternoon lets you catch the surf and cliff views the area is known for before settling in as the restaurant fills up after dark. Because it's newly opened and already generating buzz, weekend evenings should be assumed busy by default — arrive early or book ahead if you have a specific time in mind.

## Nearby & tips

Bingin's main appeal beyond the restaurant is the beach itself, reached via a steep set of stairs cut into the cliff, plus a handful of surf breaks that draw both beginners and experienced riders depending on the swell. Pair a visit to Pak Gula with a walk along the clifftop path connecting Bingin to Padang Padang Beach, one of the peninsula's most photographed coves. Given that Pak Gula is described as already busy, reservations are strongly recommended, especially for dinner or sunset-adjacent time slots — check whether they take bookings via Instagram or a reservation platform, since exact contact details and opening hours weren't confirmed here and are best verified directly before your visit. As with most of Uluwatu's dining scene, cash and cards are typically both accepted, but confirm on arrival if you're paying by card for a larger group bill.
