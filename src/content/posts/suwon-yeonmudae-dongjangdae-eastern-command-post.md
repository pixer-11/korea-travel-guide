## Why go

Yeonmudae, formally known as Dongjangdae (동장대) or the Eastern Command Post, is one of the more overlooked stops along Hwaseong Fortress, Suwon's UNESCO World Heritage-listed 18th-century defensive wall. While most visitors beeline for Hwaseong Haenggung Palace or the dramatic Paldalmun Gate, Yeonmudae was historically the fortress's military parade and training ground — where troops drilled, commanders reviewed formations, and archers practiced. Today it's a wide open plaza with a raised pavilion, flanked by mature pine trees, and it remains one of the few spots inside the fortress where visitors can actually fire a bow at a real target, giving a tactile sense of Joseon-era military life that the palace buildings alone don't provide. With a 4.5 rating across more than 3,000 reviews, it's clearly earned a loyal following among those who make the detour, even though it rarely appears on first-timer itineraries.

## Getting there

Yeonmudae sits at 20 Changnyong-daero 103beon-gil, Maehyang-dong, Paldal-gu, on the eastern stretch of the Hwaseong Fortress wall. The easiest approach is from Suwon Station or Hwaseong Haenggung: take a bus toward Yeonmudae/Dongjangdae stop (several local city buses, including routes that loop the fortress perimeter, stop within a short walk), or simply walk the fortress wall trail from Janganmun Gate heading east — it's roughly a 20–25 minute walk along the ramparts. Taxis from central Suwon (near Suwon Station or Paldalmun) typically take about 10 minutes. There's no dedicated subway station right at Yeonmudae, so most visitors combine a fortress wall walk with a bus or taxi for the final stretch, especially since the terrain includes some uphill sections.

## What to see and do

The centerpiece is the command post platform itself, a stone-and-wood structure elevated above the parade ground, from which officers once oversaw drills and reviewed troop formations. The open field in front is large enough to picture cavalry and infantry exercises taking place. Adjacent to the plaza is a functioning archery range where visitors can rent a bow and arrows for a modest fee and try hitting a traditional target — a nod to the site's original purpose and one of the most hands-on experiences available anywhere on the fortress grounds. Spend time examining the surrounding wall sections and watchtowers nearby, including views toward Hwahongmun, the ornate seven-arched floodgate spanning Suwoncheon Stream, a short walk downhill. Yeonpungmun Gate is also within easy reach for those continuing the wall circuit.

## When to go

Early morning or late afternoon are best, both for softer light on the stonework and for avoiding midday heat, since the plaza offers little shade beyond the tree line. Spring (April–May) and autumn (October–November) are the most comfortable seasons, with clear skies and the surrounding pines and foliage adding color to photos. Weekdays are noticeably quieter than weekends, when the archery range can have a short wait. If a fortress lighting or lantern event is running seasonally, an early evening visit is worth it, as parts of the wall are illuminated after dark. Plan for 30–45 minutes at Yeonmudae itself, or longer if archery is on the agenda.

## Nearby and practical tips

Hwaseong Fortress admission is inexpensive and typically covers the full wall circuit, though it's worth double-checking current pricing and hours locally since these details shift periodically. Wear real walking shoes — the connecting wall paths involve uneven stone steps and moderate inclines. Combine Yeonmudae with a walk down to Hwahongmun and, if time allows, Hwaseong Haenggung Palace for the full historical arc from military command post to royal residence. A rental of the small fortress trolley (Hwaseong-eolyeolcha) is a convenient shortcut for anyone who wants to see more of the wall without the full walk.
