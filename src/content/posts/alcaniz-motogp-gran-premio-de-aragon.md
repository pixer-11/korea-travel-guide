## Why go

MotorLand Aragón is one of the more technically demanding circuits on the MotoGP calendar, a 5.077 km track with 17 corners, blind crests and a long back straight that rewards braking and rewards riders willing to take risks. Since MotoGP first raced here in 2010, the venue has built a reputation among fans as a "riders' circuit" — less glamorous than Barcelona or Jerez, but a genuine test of skill. For visitors, the appeal is twofold: world-class racing across three classes (MotoGP, Moto2, Moto3) in one ticket, and the chance to see it in a compact, low-key Spanish town that hasn't been reshaped around tourism the way bigger race host cities have.

## Getting there

Alcañiz sits in the Teruel province of Aragón, roughly equidistant from Zaragoza (about 100 km) and Barcelona (around 300 km). Zaragoza is the most practical gateway: it has its own airport with limited routes, but the AVE high-speed rail link to Madrid and Barcelona makes Zaragoza-Delicias station the more reliable arrival point, followed by a road transfer or rental car to Alcañiz (roughly 1.5 hours by car). Barcelona's El Prat airport is a common option for international flights, with a longer drive (around 3 hours) down the A-2/A-7 corridors.

MotorLand Aragón itself is about 6 km from central Alcañiz, off the A-222 toward Andorra (Teruel). During race weekend, expect organized shuttle buses from Alcañiz and sometimes from Zaragoza — MotoGP.com and the circuit's own site publish these details closer to the event, along with designated parking zones for those driving in. Given rural road capacity, arriving early on race day (Sunday) is the standard advice from past editions; traffic out after the MotoGP race can take a while to clear.

## What to see and do

Beyond the racing, the paddock area typically allows fans (with appropriate tickets) to watch teams prepare bikes and, depending on the weekend's program, attend autograph or fan-zone sessions with riders. Grandstands wrap much of the lap, but corners like Turn 1 (a tight, uphill braking zone) and the final corner complex are popular for overtakes and worth prioritizing if general-admission viewing mounds are part of your ticket.

In Alcañiz itself, the historic quarter is built around the Plaza de España, dominated by the Colegiata de Santa María la Mayor (a Baroque collegiate church) and the Alcañiz Castle (Castillo de los Calatravos), a former Order of Calatrava stronghold that now includes a parador hotel — worth a visit even if you're not staying there, for the views over the Guadalope river and surrounding badlands landscape. The town's tapas bars around the old quarter get busy during race weekend, and it's worth booking accommodation well in advance, as Alcañiz's hotel stock is limited relative to demand during MotoGP weekend.

## When to go and what to expect

Late August in this part of Aragón is hot and dry, often into the low-to-mid 30s°C (high 80s–90s°F) during the day, so sun protection, water, and light clothing for the grandstands matter more than for cooler-weather race weekends. Evenings cool down noticeably, which suits the town's outdoor dining scene.

The event typically runs Friday through Sunday: free practice on Friday, qualifying on Saturday, and warm-up plus all three championship races on Sunday. Exact timings, ticket categories (general admission vs. grandstand vs. paddock passes) and support-race schedules are set and published by organizers closer to the date — treat any prices or timetables you see elsewhere as provisional until confirmed on the official MotoGP and MotorLand Aragón channels.

## Nearby & tips

If extending the trip, Teruel city (about an hour south) has notable Mudéjar architecture worth a detour, and Zaragoza offers a fuller city break with the Basilica del Pilar and Aljafería Palace. Given the August date, book Alcañiz lodging and any Zaragoza-Alcañiz transfers as early as possible, since rooms sell out well ahead of race weekend.
