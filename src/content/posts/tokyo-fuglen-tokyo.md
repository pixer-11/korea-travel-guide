## Why Fuglen Tokyo Stands Out

Fuglen started life in Oslo in 1963 as a furniture shop and coffee bar, and the Tokyo outpost — the brand's first location outside Norway, opened in 2012 — keeps that dual identity intact. By day it runs as a serious specialty coffee bar with rotating single-origin beans roasted in-house; by night the same room transforms into a cocktail bar with a small, inventive menu often featuring Nordic-inflected infusions and bitters. The wood-paneled interior is filled with actual vintage Scandinavian furniture, much of it sourced and for sale, giving the space a lived-in, mid-century feel that's rare in Tokyo's café scene. With a 4.3 rating across nearly 3,750 reviews, it has become a fixture for both coffee obsessives and cocktail drinkers.

## Getting There

Fuglen Tokyo sits at 1-chōme-16-11 Tomigaya, in Shibuya ward, in the quiet, leafy Tomigaya neighborhood just north of the much busier Shibuya crossing area. The closest stations are Yoyogi-Hachiman (on the Odakyu Odawara Line) and Yoyogi-Koen (on the Chiyoda Line), each roughly a 10-12 minute walk away. It's also a walkable 15-20 minutes from Shibuya Station if you don't mind cutting through the backstreets past Yoyogi Park — a pleasant route, especially in the morning. Because it's tucked into a residential-feeling stretch rather than directly on a main shopping street, it's easy to treat as a deliberate detour rather than a passing stop; most visitors pair it with a walk through Yoyogi Park or a stroll to the nearby Tomigaya and Okusawa-adjacent boutique streets.

## What to Order

Mornings lean toward the coffee side: pour-overs and espresso from Fuglen's own roastery, alongside Scandinavian baked goods — cinnamon buns (kanelboller) and cardamom buns are the standing favorites, best enjoyed with a filter coffee rather than milk-based drinks if you want to taste the roast. The pastry case sells out of popular items by mid-morning on weekends, so early risers get first pick. After dark, the space shifts into bar mode, usually from around 8pm, with a compact cocktail list that draws on Nordic ingredients like aquavit, along with classics reworked with house-made syrups and bitters. Portions and prices sit in the moderate range for Tokyo (price level 2), making it reasonable for a casual coffee stop or a slightly more considered evening drink, though exact prices shift and are worth checking on-site or on the venue's own site before visiting.

## When to Go

Weekday mornings, roughly 8-10am, are the calmest window for coffee and a pastry with a real chance at a seat. Weekend mornings draw a steady line, partly from Instagram visibility and partly from genuine local loyalty. The evening cocktail hours get busier as the night goes on, especially Thursday through Saturday, and seating is limited, so arriving right at opening for the bar service (rather than mid-evening) improves your odds of a table. Because the venue splits into two distinct modes — café by day, bar by night — it rewards a return visit if you have the time: one trip for coffee, another for cocktails, rather than trying to catch both moods at once.

## Nearby & Tips

Yoyogi Park is close enough for a pre- or post-coffee walk, particularly good in cherry blossom season (late March to early April) or during autumn foliage. The Tomigaya/Yoyogi-Hachiman area itself rewards wandering — it's known for small design shops, independent boutiques, and quiet cafés that don't attract the same crowds as Shibuya's main strip. Seating inside Fuglen is limited and mostly communal or counter-style, so larger groups may want to call ahead or expect a short wait. If buying vintage furniture pieces on display appeals to you, ask staff — much of what furnishes the room is genuinely for sale, a holdover from the brand's original Oslo concept.
