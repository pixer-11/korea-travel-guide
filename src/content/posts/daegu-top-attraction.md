## Why Daegu, and why this route

Daegu doesn't have one iconic postcard monument the way Seoul has its palaces or Busan has Haeundae Beach. Its appeal is more textured: a historic market culture, a preserved traditional medicine district found nowhere else in Korea at this scale, and a mountain temple complex on the city's northern edge. The most rewarding way to "see Daegu's top attraction" is to treat downtown Jung-gu as one connected experience rather than hunting for a single site.

## Getting there and getting around

Daegu is reachable by KTX from Seoul (around 1 hour 50 minutes to Dongdaegu Station) or from Busan (about 50 minutes). The city has two subway lines that matter most for visitors: Line 1 and Line 2, which cross at Banwoldang Station — the practical hub for everything described below. From Dongdaegu Station, a short taxi or subway transfer via Line 1 gets you into the old downtown in under 20 minutes.

## What to see: Seomun Market and the medicine district

Exit Banwoldang Station and head toward Seomun Market, one of Korea's three largest traditional markets and a place locals have shopped for centuries. By day it's textiles, hanbok fabric, and household goods; after sunset, the Seomun Market Night Market takes over an adjacent lot with street food stalls — try mayak gimbap (small, addictive seaweed rice rolls), tteokbokki, and grilled skewers. It typically runs seasonally, roughly spring through autumn, so check current dates before planning a night visit.

A ten-minute walk east brings you to Yangnyeongsi, a herbal medicine market district with roots going back to the Joseon Dynasty. Wooden shopfronts display ginseng, deer antler, and dried herbs in glass jars, and a small Yangnyeongsi Museum explains the district's history and traditional diagnostic tools — worth 20-30 minutes even if you don't read Korean, since displays include English signage.

From there, Daegu's colonial and modern history is walkable: the Gyesan Cathedral (one of Korea's oldest Gothic-style churches), the Jeil Church area, and the alleys around Jinbaek Restaurant Street mix early 20th-century buildings with contemporary cafes. Allow 2-3 hours to wander this whole downtown loop without rushing.

## Palgongsan and Donghwasa Temple for a change of pace

For a contrast to the market density, Palgongsan mountain on Daegu's northern edge holds Donghwasa Temple, founded in the Silla Dynasty and home to a towering seated Buddha statue (Tonguibul) visible from a distance. A cable car partway up the mountain shortens the climb toward Gatbawi, a rock-carved Buddha statue perched near the summit that's a popular pilgrimage and hiking destination, especially during exam season when students and parents visit to pray for good results. Reaching Palgongsan generally requires a bus transfer from downtown or a taxi (roughly 40-50 minutes), so budget half a day if you're combining the temple, the cable car, and any hiking.

## When to go and how long to stay

Spring (April) and autumn (October-November) are the most comfortable seasons — Daegu is known for hot, humid summers, sometimes nicknamed "Daegu's furnace" locally because of high summer temperatures. A single focused day covers downtown Jung-gu comfortably; add a second day if Palgongsan is on the list. Weekday mornings are quieter at Seomun Market, while evenings and weekends bring the fullest night-market atmosphere.

## Nearby and practical tips

Dongseongno, a pedestrian shopping street near Banwoldang, is a natural add-on for cafes and retail between market visits. If traveling by KTX, Dongdaegu Station has luggage storage lockers, useful if you're doing Daegu as a day trip before continuing to Busan or Gyeongju, both under an hour away by train.
