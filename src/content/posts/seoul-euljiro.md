## What to know

Euljiro (also spelled Eulji-ro) is a long district in central Seoul that has been the city's manufacturing backbone for generations. Its narrow alleys are historically packed with small family-run workshops specializing in things like tools, hardware, lighting fixtures, printing, tiles, and metalwork — many still operating out of the same cramped storefronts they've used for decades. In recent years, this rough industrial texture has attracted a wave of independent bars, cafés, and galleries that deliberately move into old buildings without changing much on the outside, giving the area its nickname among locals as "Hipjiro" (a play on "hip" and Euljiro). The result is a layered neighborhood where a grandfather welding metal parts by day might have a stylish cocktail bar operating one floor above him by night.

This contrast is exactly what makes Euljiro worth seeking out: it hasn't been redesigned for visitors, so what you see is a genuinely working part of the city, not a recreation. Signage is often minimal or entirely absent for the newer bars and cafés, since many operate as low-key, word-of-mouth spots inside nondescript industrial buildings — part of the area's appeal is the sense of discovery.

## How to get there

Euljiro is served by its own subway line stops (Euljiro 1-ga through Euljiro 4-ga, and Euljiro 3-ga station in particular is a well-known access point where multiple lines intersect), making it an easy add-on to a day exploring central Seoul. It sits within walking distance of other well-known areas like Myeongdong and Jongno, so it's easy to combine with a broader itinerary rather than treating it as a separate trip. Because the appeal is largely about wandering the alleys themselves, arriving by subway and exploring on foot is the most practical approach — there's little reason to drive, and parking in the narrow streets would be impractical anyway.

## Tips

- **Visit twice, at two different times of day.** The daytime character (working hardware and printing shops) and the nighttime character (bars and cafés) are almost entirely different scenes.
- **Don't expect clear signage.** Many of the newer venues are intentionally low-profile, with unmarked doors or entrances tucked above or behind older shops. Wandering and a bit of trial and error is part of the experience.
- **Bring cash.** Small, independent shops and older-style businesses in areas like this are less likely to be set up for card payments than larger commercial districts.
- **Wear comfortable shoes.** The alleys can be uneven, narrow, and occasionally cluttered with materials from active workshops.
- **Be respectful of working shops.** Many of the trade businesses are still functioning livelihoods, not attractions, so keep that in mind if wandering through during business hours.
