## Why Euljiro Is Worth Your Time

Euljiro sits in the heart of Jung-gu, sandwiched between Myeongdong and Jongno, but it feels like a different city. For decades this was Seoul's manufacturing backbone — block after block of tiny workshops making everything from custom lighting fixtures to printing plates, tools, and industrial hardware. Rather than being demolished for redevelopment, much of that grit survived, and in the last several years a wave of bars, coffee shops, and galleries has moved into the upper floors and back alleys of these same buildings. Locals call the phenomenon "newtro" (new + retro): a bare-concrete stairwell leads past a welding shop to a hidden bar with vintage furniture and натural wine. The contrast — industrial daytime, bohemian nighttime — is what makes Euljiro genuinely distinctive rather than a manufactured "hidden gem."

## Getting There

The district runs along Euljiro-ro, roughly between Euljiro 1-ga and Euljiro 4-ga. The most useful subway access points are:

- **Euljiro 3-ga Station** (Line 2 and Line 3) — exits 3, 4, and 8 put you closest to the densest cluster of bars and workshop-alleys, including the area locals nickname "Hip-jiro."
- **Euljiro 4-ga Station** (Line 2 and Line 5) — good for reaching the tool and hardware markets and the printing-shop streets further east.
- **Eulji-ro 1-ga Station** (Line 2) — closer to the Euljiro entrance nearest Myeongdong and City Hall, useful if you're combining a visit with those areas.

Because the interesting parts of Euljiro are the narrow side alleys off the main road, plug in the station exit rather than a specific address — then walk and look for stairwells with hand-painted signs.

## What to See, Do, and Eat

Spend an hour simply walking the alleys between Euljiro 3-ga and 4-ga during daylight to see the workshop culture still in operation: metal shops, lighting fixture makers, and tile suppliers with merchandise spilling onto the sidewalk. Many of these craftsmen have operated from the same storefronts for 40-plus years.

As evening falls, the same blocks reveal small bars and cafés on the second and third floors of nondescript buildings — look for QR-code menus, warm lighting through frosted glass, or a cluster of people waiting near an unmarked door. This is intentional; many venues keep a low profile.

For food, Euljiro is also known for old-school Korean comfort dishes rather than trendy fusion. Look for **hoehoe-jang (raw beef) alleys** and unassuming *sikdang* (diners) serving **kalguksu** (knife-cut noodle soup), **golbaengi muchim** (spicy whelk salad, a classic pairing with soju), and grilled offal at plastic-stool pojangmacha-style spots. Portions and prices tend to be aimed at office workers, so it's inexpensive by Seoul standards.

Adjacent points of reference: **Gwangjang Market**, one of Seoul's oldest traditional markets (famous for bindaetteok and mayak gimbap), is a short walk northeast near Jongno 5-ga, and **Cheonggyecheon Stream** runs parallel to Euljiro just to the north, making an easy connector walk toward Dongdaemun.

## When to Go and How Long to Stay

Evenings (roughly 6–10 pm) show Euljiro at its best, when the bar scene comes alive but workshops are still visibly closing up for the night, giving you both faces of the neighborhood. Weeknights are noticeably quieter and easier for exploring narrow alleys; weekends bring longer waits at the smaller bars. Two to three hours is enough for a first visit — an hour of daytime-style wandering plus dinner and a drink. Avoid mid-afternoon on Sundays, when many workshops and some bars are closed.

## Practical Tips

Wear shoes suited to uneven pavement and stairwells, since many bars are up narrow, unmarked staircases with no elevator. Cash still helps at old-school diners, though card acceptance has grown. Because the area lacks obvious signage, using a mapping app for the general block and then exploring on foot works better than trying to find an exact pinned address.
