## Why Coffee Street Exists

Gangneung's reputation as Korea's coffee capital isn't marketing spin — it dates back to the 1980s, when instant coffee vending machines lined Anmok Beach and locals began experimenting with roasting. By the 2000s, small-batch roasters had set up shop directly on the sand-facing road at Anmok, and the area organically became known as "Coffee Street" (커피거리). Gangneung also hosts an annual Coffee Festival in autumn, cementing the city's identity around bean culture. Today the street is a dense row of multi-story cafés, most with floor-to-ceiling windows facing the East Sea, making it as much a scenery stop as a caffeine stop.

## Getting There

From Seoul, the KTX-equivalent high-speed rail (KTX-Gangneung Line) runs directly from Seoul Station or Cheongnyangni Station to Gangneung Station in roughly 2 to 2.5 hours — this is the fastest and most comfortable option. Intercity buses from Seoul's Express Bus Terminal also run frequently and take about 2.5–3 hours depending on traffic.

From Gangneung Station or the bus terminal, Anmok Coffee Street is not walkable — take a local city bus (several routes head toward Anmok Beach) or a taxi, which typically takes 10–15 minutes and costs a modest fare. Many visitors combine the trip with Gyeongpo Beach and Gyeongpodae Pavilion, since Anmok and Gyeongpo Beach are connected by a scenic 2-3 km pine forest boardwalk along the coastline — a pleasant 30-40 minute walk if the weather's good.

## What to See and Do

The street itself is the attraction: a line of independent and multi-floor café buildings, many owned by pioneering Gangneung roasters, stacked with rooftop terraces and floor-to-ceiling glass facing the water. The point isn't to pick one famous shop — it's to wander, compare views, and pick whichever rooftop or window seat has the best angle on the waves. Signature things to try include hand-drip single-origin pours (Gangneung roasters take pride in filter coffee over espresso drinks), and seasonal specialties like iced coffee with local specialties such as injeolmi (rice cake) flavoring, which some shops fold into lattes.

Beyond coffee, walk the sand at Anmok Beach itself — it's less crowded than Gyeongpo and has a long pedestrian promenade good for photos of the cafés backing onto the shoreline. If you continue along the coastal boardwalk toward Gyeongpo, you'll pass Gyeongpo Beach, one of the east coast's most popular swimming beaches in summer, and can detour inland slightly to see Gyeongpodae Pavilion, a historic pavilion overlooking Gyeongpo Lake, one of the "eight scenic views" of the Gangwon region.

## Best Time to Visit

Late afternoon into early evening is ideal — the sun angles low over the water, cafés fill with golden light, and you can transition straight into sunset viewing without changing location. Weekdays are noticeably quieter than weekends, when the street draws heavy domestic tourist traffic, especially in summer (July–August) when nearby beaches are packed. Spring (April–May) and autumn (September–October) offer milder weather for the coastal walk and fewer crowds. If your visit lines up with the autumn Gangneung Coffee Festival, expect pop-up stalls, tastings, and a livelier but busier atmosphere.

## Practical Tips

Plan for 2-3 hours minimum if you want to sit down, enjoy a drink with a view, and walk part of the boardwalk. There's no admission fee for the street — you're simply paying café prices per drink, which vary shop to shop, so treat it like grazing rather than a single fixed-cost attraction. Comfortable footwear helps for the boardwalk section, and a light jacket is worth carrying even in warmer months since coastal wind picks up by evening. Cash and card are both generally accepted, but as with any small independent café, having some cash on hand avoids friction at smaller counters. Pair the visit with Gangneung's Ojukheon House (birthplace of scholar Yi I) or the Jumunjin fish market if you have a full day to spend in the area.
