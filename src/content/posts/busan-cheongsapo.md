## What to know

Cheongsapo sits between Haeundae Beach and Songjeong Beach on Busan's eastern coastline, and it's one of the few spots in the city where a genuine, still-active fishing harbor exists alongside a wave of newer cafés and small restaurants. The name itself reflects its identity as a seaside village ("po" meaning harbor or cove), and locals and visitors alike come here less for a beach and more for the atmosphere: boats bobbing in the harbor, drying nets and seafood stalls, and a coastline dotted with cafés built specifically to take advantage of open sea views.

In recent years the neighborhood has become known as a trendy destination, largely because of this contrast — an old-school fishing community that hasn't been erased, but instead now hosts a growing number of café terraces, rooftop seating, and photogenic coastal architecture. The area is compact enough to explore in an hour or two, but many visitors linger longer to sit with a drink and watch the water.

The coastline near Cheongsapo is also part of a broader scenic corridor connecting to Haeundae's Dongbaek Island (Nurimaru) and continuing along the coast toward Songjeong, so it works well as a walking link between beach neighborhoods rather than an isolated stop.

## How to get there

Cheongsapo is most easily reached from Haeundae, Busan's main coastal transit hub. From Haeundae Station or the Haeundae Beach area, buses and taxis run along the coastal road toward this stretch of shoreline; a taxi ride from Haeundae is short and straightforward, while local buses that follow the coastal route also stop in the area. Because it sits along a coastal walking path, many visitors combine a visit here with a longer stroll from Haeundae or Dongbaek Island, arriving on foot rather than by vehicle.

As with most of Busan, having a T-money transit card (usable on subway and buses) makes getting to and from the coast simple, and taxis are a reliable backup if you're carrying beach or café gear and don't want to wait for a bus.

## Tips

- Go in the late afternoon or toward sunset — the ocean-facing cafés and harbor views are at their best with low light over the water.
- Wear comfortable shoes if you plan to combine Cheongsapo with the coastal walking path toward Haeundae or Songjeong; parts of the route involve stairs and uneven harbor-side paths.
- Bring cash for small seafood stalls and older, family-run spots near the harbor, even though many newer cafés accept cards.
- Weekends and holiday afternoons draw crowds to the more scenic café terraces, so arriving earlier in the day or on a weekday gives a quieter experience.
- Because this is a working harbor, be mindful around boats, nets, and fishing equipment — it's a real workplace as much as a scenic backdrop.
