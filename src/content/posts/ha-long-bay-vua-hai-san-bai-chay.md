## Why go

Ha Long Bay's tourist core is saturated with seafood restaurants aimed squarely at cruise-ship crowds, and prices/quality vary wildly. Vựa Hải Sản Bãi Cháy stands out because it operates on the market-style model common in Vietnamese coastal cities: tanks and ice trays of live seafood out front, priced by weight, which you select yourself before it's carried to the kitchen. This transparency is a big part of why it has built up 1,684+ reviews and a 4.6 average — diners can see exactly what they're paying for rather than trusting a printed menu with vague "seasonal price" listings. It's a restaurant for travelers who want an unpretentious, high-volume, genuinely local seafood experience rather than a hotel buffet or set cruise-boat meal.

## Getting there

The restaurant sits in Bai Chay, the tourist and hotel district on the western side of Ha Long City, across the Bai Chay Bridge from the older Hon Gai side. Bai Chay is where most travelers already base themselves, since it holds the bulk of hotels, the night market, and the ferry/cruise departure piers on Ha Long Bay. Because the address resolves to a Plus Code (XX5W+VMR) rather than a numbered street, the simplest approach is to drop the name directly into Google Maps or Grab and let it route you — this is standard for Bai Chay, where many businesses sit just off the main tourist strip without conventional street numbering. A taxi or Grab car from most Bai Chay hotels should take well under 15 minutes; walking is possible if you're staying near the Bai Chay night market or beachfront, though seafood restaurants of this type are usually set back from the main waterfront road.

## What to see / eat

Expect a live-tank setup: mud crab, tiger prawns, king crab (seasonal), scallops, clams, squid, and reef fish are typically displayed on ice or in tanks so you can choose the exact animal or weight before ordering. Standard Vietnamese coastal preparations apply — expect options like garlic-butter or tamarind-sauce prawns, salt-and-pepper squid, steamed clams with lemongrass, and grilled scallops with scallion oil, priced per kilogram or per portion depending on the item. Because pricing is weight-based and seafood costs fluctuate daily, ask staff to confirm the current per-kg rate and have them weigh your selection in front of you before it goes to the kitchen — this is standard practice at market-style seafood spots in Vietnam and avoids surprises on the bill. Portions here tend to suit groups of 3 or more sharing several dishes family-style rather than solo diners ordering a single plate.

## When to go

Early to mid-evening (roughly 6–7:30pm) is the sweet spot: seafood restaurants like this restock through the afternoon, so tanks are fullest and freshest before the dinner rush thins them out. Lunchtime is quieter and can work well if you're touring Bai Chay before or after a bay cruise, but the fullest selection and liveliest atmosphere are typically after sunset. Weekends and Vietnamese holiday periods (Tet, national holidays) bring heavier crowds and can mean a short wait, so arriving right at opening in the evening helps secure a table without a queue.

## Nearby & tips

Bai Chay's night market and waterfront promenade are within easy reach for a post-dinner walk, and the Bai Chay cable car / Sun World Ha Long Complex is a short ride away if you want to pair the meal with an evening view over the bay. Since menus at seafood-by-weight restaurants rarely list fixed prices for premium items like king crab, it's worth setting a rough per-person budget with your group before ordering multiple large dishes. A translation app helps if staff-facing menus are in Vietnamese only, though picking seafood by pointing at the tank works universally. As with any live-seafood restaurant, ask which items are freshest that day — staff will usually steer larger groups toward what just arrived rather than what's been sitting longest.
