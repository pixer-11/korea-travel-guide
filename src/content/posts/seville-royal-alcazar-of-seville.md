## Why go

The Royal Alcázar of Seville isn't a ruin or a museum piece pretending at royal life — it's a working palace, still used by the Spanish royal family when they're in the city, which makes it unique among Spain's great monuments. Its centerpiece, the Palacio de Don Pedro, was built in the 1360s by Christian King Pedro I using Moorish craftsmen from Granada, producing a building that's technically Christian but visually and structurally Mudéjar: horseshoe arches, carved stucco, gilded ceilings, and acres of 16th-century azulejo tilework. Add in the Gothic Palace, the Admiral's Hall (where voyages to the Americas were once planned), and the sprawling gardens with fountains, pavilions, and peacocks, and it's easy to see why it holds a 4.7 rating across more than 100,000 reviews. It's also a filming location for "Game of Thrones" (as the Water Gardens of Dorne), which draws a subset of visitors specifically hunting for those spots.

## Getting there

The Alcázar sits in the Casco Antiguo (old town), directly beside Seville Cathedral and the Giralda tower, at the edge of the Barrio Santa Cruz. There's no metro station right at the door — Seville's compact center is best covered on foot — but the closest tram stop is Archivo de Indias on the T1 line, about a 3-minute walk. If arriving from Santa Justa train station, a taxi or the C1/C2 bus into the center followed by a 15–20 minute walk covers it easily. The entrance is on Plaza del Triunfo, the same square that fronts the Cathedral, so most visitors simply combine the two on foot from wherever they're staying in the old town.

## What to see and how to plan your visit

Buy tickets online in advance with a specific entry time slot — this is the single most important piece of practical advice, since walk-up lines in high season can mean waits of an hour or more, and tickets do sell out days ahead in spring and fall. Morning slots (the palace typically opens around 9:30am) are cooler and less crowded than midday.

Inside, prioritize:
- **Palacio de Don Pedro** — the Mudéjar heart of the complex, including the Patio de las Doncellas (Courtyard of the Maidens) with its sunken garden and double-height arcade added in the 16th century.
- **Salón de Embajadores** (Ambassadors' Hall) — a domed ceremonial room with a spectacular wooden half-orange dome overhead.
- **Patio del Yeso**, one of the oldest surviving sections, predating Pedro I's rebuild.
- **The Gardens** — far larger than most first-time visitors expect; look for the Galería de Grutesco (a raised gallery along an old city wall), the Mercury Pool, and the maze-like Jardín de las Danzas.
- **The Royal Apartments** (Cuarto Real Alto), an optional additional-fee, separately timed ticket that shows the rooms still used by the royal family today — worth it for anyone specifically interested in the monarchy's continued presence here.

Budget 2.5 to 3 hours minimum; garden-lovers and photographers often stay closer to 4.

## When to go and what to expect

March through May and October through early November offer the best balance of mild weather and manageable crowds. Summer (June–August) brings intense heat — Seville regularly hits 38–40°C (100°F+) in July and August — so an early morning slot is close to essential, and the gardens offer little shade at midday. Winter is the quietest season and a good option for a slower, less crowded visit, though some garden areas are less lush.

## Nearby & tips

Because the Alcázar and Cathedral share the same plaza, most visitors do both in a single day — consider Cathedral/Giralda first thing, then the Alcázar, or reverse the order depending on your ticket time. The Barrio Santa Cruz, Seville's old Jewish quarter, is immediately adjacent and worth wandering afterward for tapas and shade. Confirm current opening hours, ticket prices, and any Royal Apartments availability on the official Alcázar website before your trip, since these details shift seasonally.
