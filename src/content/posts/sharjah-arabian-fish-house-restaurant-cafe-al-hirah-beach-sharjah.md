## Why go

Arabian Fish House sits directly on Al Hirah (also spelled Al Heera) Beach in Sharjah's Al Fisht/Alheera Suburb area, and its 4.9 rating across roughly 7,900 reviews puts it among the most consistently praised seafood spots in the emirate. It's a Seafood Restaurant in the straightforward sense: you pick your fish, it's weighed, grilled or fried to order, and served with rice, salads, and bread while you look out at the Gulf. The price level is moderate (level 2 out of 4), so it sits comfortably between a quick beachside snack stand and a formal fine-dining seafood house — the kind of place where a family or small group can eat well without a special-occasion budget.

## Getting there

The restaurant is located at Cluster 2, Venue 6, Al Muntazah Street, in the Al Fisht/Alheera Suburb, right along Al Hirah Beach. It's a short drive from Sharjah's central corniche area and from Sharjah International Airport (roughly 15-20 minutes by car depending on traffic), making it an easy stop whether you're staying in Sharjah city or coming over from Dubai's northern suburbs, which are typically 20-30 minutes away via Sheikh Mohammed Bin Zayed Road or the Emirates Road. Public transport options are limited in this part of Sharjah, so a taxi, ride-hailing app, or rental car is the practical choice — confirm with your driver that you mean Al Hirah/Al Heera Beach, since transliterations vary. There's typically parking available near the beach cluster, but arrive a little early on weekends when the waterfront gets busy with strollers and families.

## What to eat

The draw here is whole fresh fish — commonly hammour, kingfish (chanad), and shrimp — displayed on ice and sold by weight, then cooked to your preference: grilled, fried, or in a spiced masala-style sauce. Order it "Sayadieh" style if you want the classic Gulf preparation of fish over spiced rice, or ask for it simply grilled with lemon and garlic if you'd rather taste the fish itself. Sides typically include a fattoush or tabbouleh salad, garlic sauce, and Arabic bread, and many tables share a mixed platter rather than ordering individually. Because pricing depends on the fish's weight and market rate that day, treat any number you hear in advance as a rough guide — ask staff to confirm the per-kilo rate before they cook it, which is standard practice at pay-by-weight seafood restaurants throughout the Gulf.

## The setting

Part of what elevates this beyond a standard seafood grill is the location right on Al Hirah Beach — outdoor and semi-outdoor seating lets you eat with a sea view and catch the breeze, which matters most in cooler months. The stretch of coastline here is quieter and more local in feel than Dubai's beachfront strips, giving diners a relaxed, unhurried pace even though the restaurant itself does brisk business.

## When to go

Cooler months — roughly November through March — are the most comfortable for outdoor seaside dining in Sharjah, when evening temperatures make sitting by the water genuinely pleasant rather than a heat-endurance exercise. In summer, an air-conditioned indoor table or a very early or late sitting is the more realistic plan. Evenings around sunset are popular for obvious reasons, so if you want a table without a wait, early lunch (around noon-1pm) or dinner right at opening tends to be calmer.

## How to visit like a local

This is a well-known, heavily reviewed restaurant rather than a hidden find, so the smart move is managing timing rather than seeking secrecy. Weekend evenings (Thursday-Saturday nights, plus Friday afternoon) draw the biggest crowds; a weekday lunch or an early dinner around 6pm is your best shot at walking in without a long wait. Most Gulf seafood-by-weight restaurants don't take detailed reservations for specific fish selections since the catch and pricing vary daily, so calling ahead mainly confirms opening hours rather than securing a table — walking in remains standard practice. Cash and card are both typically accepted, and a modest tip (rounding up the bill or leaving 10% for good service) is appreciated though not obligatory, in line with general UAE dining norms. The most common visitor mistake is over-ordering fish by weight without asking the per-kilo price first — always confirm cost before the fish goes on the grill, and consider sharing a mixed catch platter across the table rather than each person ordering separately, which is both more economical and more in keeping with how the dish is meant to be eaten.
