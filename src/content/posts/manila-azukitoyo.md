## Why Azukitoyo Is Worth Seeking Out

Manila's dining scene has no shortage of dessert cafés, but Azukitoyo is a different animal entirely. It's a kakigori bar — the Japanese art of finely shaved ice, layered with syrups, creams, and often fruit or bean-based toppings — reimagined through a distinctly Filipino lens. The project comes from Toyo Eatery, the acclaimed modern Filipino restaurant known for reinterpreting local heritage dishes with a chef's precision, working in collaboration with Florilège, the celebrated fine-dining restaurant in Tokyo. That pairing alone signals the level of craft involved: this isn't shaved ice as a quick cooling snack, but as a considered dessert course, built with the same rigor you'd expect from a tasting menu.

At the helm is Samantha Constantino, formerly of Inatô, another respected name in Manila's contemporary Filipino food movement. Watching her prepare each kakigori tableside is part of the draw — the shaving, layering, and plating happen in front of guests rather than arriving pre-made from a back kitchen, turning dessert into a small piece of theater.

## What Makes It Stand Out

Kakigori itself is still a novelty in Manila. While shaved ice and halo-halo have long been part of Filipino food culture, the Japanese style — ultra-fine, almost snow-like ice shavings that melt on contact — is a texture most local diners haven't experienced before. Azukitoyo's concept bridges the two traditions: Japanese technique applied to Filipino ingredients and flavor memories, rather than a straight import of a Tokyo dessert bar.

Because the menu draws on local produce and flavor references, expect it to evolve seasonally, much as Toyo Eatery's main menu does. Rather than expecting a fixed lineup, come with curiosity — part of the appeal is that the offerings are designed to shift and surprise.

## Getting There and Practical Notes

Azukitoyo operates under the Toyo Eatery group, so it's worth checking their official social channels or website for the current address, hours, and whether reservations are required, since pop-up-style and collaborative concepts like this can have limited runs or shifting schedules. Given the buzz around the Toyo Eatery x Florilège pairing, walk-ins may face a wait, especially on weekends — reserving ahead, if the system allows it, is the safer bet.

If it's located near Toyo Eatery's existing footprint in Metro Manila, expect a compact, design-conscious space rather than a large café — kakigori bars typically seat a limited number of guests at a counter or a few small tables, since the shaving and plating process is meant to be watched up close.

- **Time your visit for a weekday afternoon** if you want a calmer, less rushed experience and more attention from the chef preparing each dish.
- **Come with an open palate.** Because the concept fuses Japanese shaved-ice technique with Filipino flavor inspirations, don't expect familiar halo-halo toppings — this is closer to a dessert tasting than a street-food treat.
- **Check social media before you go.** New, collaborative concepts like this often post daily availability, special flavor drops, or temporary closures rather than fixed permanent hours.
- **Pair the visit with Toyo Eatery itself** if timing allows — since Azukitoyo comes from the same team, a meal beforehand offers a fuller sense of the kitchen's culinary philosophy.
