## Why go

Unlike restored "folk villages" built purely for tourists, Nagan Eupseong is a genuinely inhabited settlement enclosed by a Joseon-era fortress wall dating to the late 14th and early 15th centuries. Around 100 households still farm, garden, and keep house inside the walls, so the thatched-roof homes, stone-lined alleys, and vegetable patches you see are lived-in, not staged. The village retains its original administrative layout, including a government office (dongheon), a Confucian school area, and preserved sections of the granite fortress wall you can actually walk along — a rarity among Korea's folk villages. It's a low-key, unhurried counterpart to more famous sites like Hahoe or Yangdong, and its rating (4.3 from over 6,500 reviews) reflects steady appreciation from visitors who value that authenticity.

## Getting there

Nagan Eupseong sits at 6-4 Pyeongchon-ri, Nagan-myeon, in Suncheon, Jeollanam-do — roughly 30 minutes by car or intercity bus from downtown Suncheon and Suncheon Station (KTX and regular trains connect here from Seoul, Yeosu, and Gwangju). From Suncheon's intercity bus terminal, regional buses run to Nagan-myeon several times a day; ask staff for the bus heading toward "Nagan Eupseong" or "Nagan-myeon," as schedules are limited compared to city routes, so check current times before heading out. Driving is the most flexible option — there's a parking area near the entrance — and a taxi from Suncheon Station takes about 25–30 minutes. If you're combining this with Suncheon Bay National Garden or the Suncheonman Bay Wetland Reserve, renting a car for the day makes logistics far easier, since both sites are spread across the city's outskirts.

## What to see and do

Enter through one of the restored gates and climb onto the fortress wall — it circles the village for roughly 1.4 km and offers elevated views over the thatched roofs and surrounding rice paddies. Inside, wander the packed-earth lanes past private homes (many still occupied, so be respectful of doorways and yards), the old government office building, a village well, and preserved trees said to be centuries old. Look for the small workshops where locals demonstrate traditional crafts like straw weaving, pottery, or natural dyeing — these are usually seasonal, so availability varies. Traditional performances, including pansori or folk music, are sometimes staged in an open courtyard area, particularly on weekends and around Korean holidays. Food stalls and small restaurants within or near the village serve regional Jeollanam-do fare — expect dishes built around local rice, wild greens (namul), and makgeolli rice wine. Give yourself at least 90 minutes to see the fortress wall and main lanes, and up to half a day if you want to linger, try a craft activity, or catch a scheduled performance.

## When to go

Spring (April–May) and autumn (September–November) bring the mildest weather and the most photogenic light on the thatched roofs, especially early morning before tour groups arrive. Summer is humid and can be crowded with domestic tourists during school holidays, while winter is quieter but colder, with fewer craft demonstrations running. Weekday visits generally mean thinner crowds and a better chance to see residents going about ordinary chores rather than posing for photos.

## Nearby & tips

Nagan Eupseong pairs naturally with a Suncheon day trip: the Suncheon Bay Wetland Reserve (one of Korea's premier tidal flat ecosystems) and Suncheon Bay National Garden are both roughly 30–40 minutes away by car. Bring cash for small vendor stalls, as card acceptance can be inconsistent in the smaller shops. Wear shoes suited to uneven stone and dirt paths, particularly if you plan to walk the full fortress wall circuit. Because people actually live here, keep noise down near residential doorways and ask before photographing individuals up close.
