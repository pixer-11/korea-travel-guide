## What to know

Busan's identity as Korea's largest port city is inseparable from its seafood culture, and nowhere captures that better than the area around Jagalchi Market. This is a working fish market first and a tourist attraction second — vendors sell live fish, shellfish, octopus, and countless varieties of catch straight from tanks and ice-packed crates. The typical experience is to pick out what looks good at a ground-floor stall, agree on a price, and then have it cut and served as raw sashimi (hoe) or brought to a nearby seating area or upstairs eatery to be cooked. It's a hands-on, sensory way to eat that rewards visitors who are curious and flexible rather than looking for a quiet sit-down meal.

Beyond the market itself, the surrounding neighborhood and Busan's coastline in general are known for a wide range of seafood dishes — raw fish platters, spicy seafood stews, grilled shellfish, and dried seafood snacks sold by street vendors. Menus and offerings shift with the season and the day's catch, so part of the appeal is not knowing exactly what will be freshest until you arrive.

## How to get there

Jagalchi Market sits near Busan's downtown waterfront and is reachable via the Busan Metro, with a station named after the market itself on one of the main lines. From there, it's a short walk to the market building and the surrounding streets of stalls. Because this is a central, well-known landmark, taxis and rideshare drivers will also recognize the name immediately, which is useful if you're coming from accommodation elsewhere in the city or arriving with luggage.

The market area is large and spread across multiple sections — an indoor market building, an outdoor street market, and rows of restaurants — so it helps to allow extra time simply to walk through and see what's on offer before committing to a stall.

## Tips

- **Bring cash.** Many small vendors in traditional markets like this prefer or require cash, and it makes negotiating prices for live seafood far simpler.
- **Ask before you sit.** If you buy fish on the ground floor and want it prepared, confirm with the vendor whether there's a table-use or preparation fee, and whether you're expected to eat upstairs or at an attached seating area.
- **Go with an appetite and an open mind.** Portions are often meant for sharing, and the freshest, best-value items are usually whatever's most abundant that day rather than a fixed menu.
- **Language basics help.** Pointing at tanks and using simple gestures works fine, but knowing a few basic Korean numbers or phrases for ordering can smooth the process.
- **Visit at a lively hour.** Markets are generally busiest and most vibrant in the morning through early afternoon, when the catch is freshest and stalls are fully stocked.
- **Combine it with a waterfront walk.** The surrounding harbor area makes for a pleasant stroll before or after eating, and it's easy to pair a market visit with other nearby Busan waterfront attractions.
