## Why Busan Is Korea's Seafood City

Busan grew up around its harbor, and the city's food culture still runs on what comes off the boats each morning. Unlike Seoul, where seafood restaurants are scattered across neighborhoods, Busan concentrates the experience in a handful of markets and coastal districts where you can watch the catch move from tank to table within the hour. The signature style here is **hoe** (Korean-style raw fish, sliced thicker than Japanese sashimi and eaten with ssamjang and perilla leaves), alongside grilled shellfish, spicy fish stews, and the region's own specialties like dwaeji gukbap's seafood cousin, milmyeon, served cold in summer.

## Jagalchi Market: The Essential Stop

No seafood guide to Busan skips **Jagalchi Market**, Korea's largest seafood market, on the harbor in Nampo-dong. Take Busan Metro Line 1 to Jagalchi Station and exit 10, then walk about five minutes toward the water. The market operates on two levels: the ground floor and outdoor stalls sell live fish, octopus, abalone, and shellfish straight from tanks, while the upper floors of the main Jagalchi building house restaurants that will prepare whatever you buy downstairs for a per-person cooking fee. This "buy downstairs, eat upstairs" system is the classic Jagalchi experience. Look for **agujjim** (spicy braised monkfish) and **ganjang gejang** (soy-marinated raw crab) as well as raw hoe platters. Mornings are best for freshness and fewer crowds; the market is busiest and liveliest by late morning through early afternoon.

## Beyond Jagalchi: Where Locals Actually Eat

A short walk from Jagalchi, **BIFF Square** and the surrounding Nampo-dong alleys are worth wandering for street-food seafood like grilled squid and fish cakes (eomuk), a Busan specialty in its own right — look for eomuk skewers served in hot broth, sold from small storefronts near Bupyeong Market. For a different seafood scene entirely, head to **Millak Waterside Park** in Suyeong-gu, near Gwangalli Beach. This strip of raw fish restaurants faces the Gwangan Bridge, and many venues let diners pick live fish from tanks with harbor views as a backdrop — arguably a more scenic (if slightly pricier) alternative to Jagalchi. Take Metro Line 2 to Millak Station, exit 3, and walk toward the water. Further east, **Gijang**, a coastal district reachable by the Donghae Line commuter rail, is renowned for raw sea urchin (seongge) and, in season (roughly October to February), the prized local delicacy of live octopus and snow crab.

## When to Go and What's in Season

Seafood in Busan follows a calendar. Winter (December–February) is peak season for snow crab and oysters; spring brings sweet shrimp and rockfish; summer is best for eel and abalone; autumn ushers in saury (kkongchi) and blue crab. If timing a trip around a specific dish, ask which season it runs in — vendors at Jagalchi are generally happy to tell you what's freshest that day. Weekday mornings offer a calmer, more authentic market atmosphere, while weekends bring domestic tourist crowds and slightly inflated prices at some stalls.

## Practical Tips for Ordering

At market-style restaurants, pricing is typically by weight or by the tank, and it is standard practice to ask the price before selecting your fish — a phrase like "eolmayeyo?" (how much is it?) goes a long way even without fluent Korean. The cooking/service fee charged by upstairs restaurants at Jagalchi is separate from the fish price itself, so budget for both. Menus at heavily touristed spots often have English or picture options, but smaller family-run stalls may not, so pointing at the tank works fine. Cash and card are both widely accepted at established market restaurants, though smaller street stalls may prefer cash. Plan for at least 90 minutes to properly browse Jagalchi and eat, and pair the visit with a walk to nearby **Gukje Market** or the **Busan Tower** area in Yongdusan Park, both within easy walking distance of Nampo-dong.
