## Why go

Meiji Jingu was built in 1920 to enshrine the deified spirits of Emperor Meiji and Empress Shoken, the imperial couple credited with steering Japan into the modern era. What makes it remarkable isn't just the shrine buildings — rebuilt in the 1950s after wartime bombing destroyed the originals — but the forest around them. Over 100,000 trees, donated from across Japan at the shrine's founding, were deliberately planted to mature into a self-sustaining woodland, and a century later it feels like ancient wilderness rather than a public park. With a 4.6 rating across more than 51,000 reviews, it's one of Tokyo's most consistently praised sights, and unlike many temples in the city, it costs nothing to walk the grounds.

## Getting there

The shrine sits in Shibuya ward at 1-1 Yoyogikamizonochō, straddling the border of Harajuku and Yoyogi. The easiest approach is from JR Harajuku Station (Yamanote Line) — exit toward Omotesando and you'll see the first giant torii gate almost immediately. Alternatively, Meiji-Jingumae Station on the Chiyoda and Fukutoku-toshin subway lines drops you at Exit 2, a short walk from the same entrance. A second, quieter southern entrance connects to Yoyogi Park, useful if you're combining both in one visit. Both main gates lead onto a long gravel path shaded by camphor and cypress trees — this walk alone takes 15-20 minutes each way, so factor that into your visit.

## What to see

The main draw is the Honden (main hall), reached after passing under a second massive torii — one of Japan's largest, rebuilt from Taiwanese cypress. Visitors approach the inner shrine through the O-torii gate and courtyard, where you can watch traditional Shinto weddings on weekends, purchase an ema (wooden wish plaque) or omamori (amulet), or write a wish on a small wooden token displayed near a wall stacked with sake barrels donated by breweries nationwide. Just north of the main complex is the Meiji Jingu Inner Garden (Gyoen), a separate paid area home to the seasonal iris garden mentioned in most reviews — roughly 1,500 iris plants bloom here in June, and the garden also holds Kiyomasa's Well, a natural spring said to have been dug by a 17th-century general. In autumn the maples along the inner paths turn color, and in early January the shrine draws several million visitors for hatsumode, the first shrine visit of the New Year — worth witnessing from a distance but not for anyone hoping for solitude.

## When to go

Arrive right at opening — the shrine gates open roughly with sunrise and close around sunset, varying by season — for the best chance at quiet paths and soft light through the canopy. Weekday mornings are noticeably calmer than weekends, when wedding processions and tour groups fill the courtyard. Early June suits the iris garden; November brings the best foliage. Avoid New Year's week entirely unless the crowd itself is the experience you want.

## Nearby & tips

Combine the visit with Yoyogi Park next door, a favorite spot for picnics and, on Sundays, amateur musicians and rockabilly dancers near the park's Harajuku-side entrance. Takeshita Street's youth-fashion chaos and the upscale boutiques of Omotesando are both a five-minute walk from Harajuku Station, making it easy to pair the shrine's calm with Tokyo's louder side in a single afternoon. Wear comfortable shoes for the gravel paths, and note that the Inner Garden charges a small entry fee separate from the free main shrine grounds — bring cash, as card payment isn't guaranteed at every kiosk. Photography is generally permitted outdoors but restricted inside the Honden itself, so check posted signs before raising a camera near the inner sanctuary.
