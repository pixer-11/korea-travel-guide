## What to eat

Expect the backbone of Thai-Chinese street noodle culture: bowls built on either egg noodles (bami), flat rice noodles (sen yai), or thin rice noodles (sen lek), served either "haeng" (dry, tossed in sauce) or "nam" (in broth). Common companions at stalls of this caliber include moo daeng (red barbecued pork), moo grob (crispy pork belly), fish balls, wontons, and a scattering of Chinese greens or morning glory. A properly made broth here should taste of long-simmered pork or chicken bones rather than bouillon powder — a hallmark of noodle stalls that earn near-perfect ratings over hundreds of reviews. Ask for the table condiment tray (sugar, chili flakes, fish sauce, vinegar with chilies) to season your bowl Thai-style rather than expecting it pre-seasoned. Portions at street-format kitchens are usually sized for one, so if you're hungry, consider ordering a side of pork satay or fried dumplings to share.

Aim for either an early lunch around 11:00-11:30am or after the dinner rush cools around 8:30-9pm. Midday (12-1pm) and prime dinner (7-8:30pm) are when nearby office workers, tour groups, and evening diners converge on well-reviewed street kitchens, and seating at these operations is often limited to a handful of tables or stools. Pattaya's tropical climate means daytime heat peaks between noon and 3pm, so an open-air noodle stop is more comfortable in the cooler morning or evening hours. November through February is Pattaya's dry, relatively cool high season and the most pleasant stretch for wandering to and from a street-food meal; April is the hottest month and best paired with an air-conditioned break before or after eating.
