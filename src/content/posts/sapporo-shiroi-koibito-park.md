## Why go

Shiroi Koibito ("White Lover") is the name of Hokkaido's most famous souvenir cookie — a thin white-chocolate sandwich biscuit made by the Sapporo confectioner Ishiya since 1976. The company built this theme park around its factory so visitors can watch the actual production line, learn the history of chocolate in Europe and Japan through a quirky collection of antique tins, tools, and packaging, and then buy the cookies fresh at a lower price than the airport gift shops. It's a hidden gem precisely because most travelers only encounter the cookie in its blue tin at Narita or New Chitose Airport, never realizing there's a whole mock-Tudor complex behind it with a clock tower, rose garden, and toy museum tucked into a quiet residential corner of Nishi Ward.

## Getting there

The park sits at 2-chōme-11-36 Miyanosawa 2 Jō, Nishi Ward, about 20–25 minutes by subway from central Sapporo. Take the Tozai Line westbound to Miyanosawa Station (the line's western terminus), then either walk roughly 15–20 minutes north or catch a local bus toward Shiroi Koibito Park — signage in English makes the route easy to follow once you exit the station. Drivers can use the on-site parking lot, though spaces fill up on weekends and during summer school holidays. There is no direct JR rail access, so the subway-plus-walk combination is the most reliable route for first-timers.

## What to see and do

The self-guided tour winds through several themed rooms: a factory-viewing corridor where you can watch Shiroi Koibito cookies actually being wrapped and packed on the production line, a "Chocolate Academy" hall tracing cacao's journey from Mesoamerica to Europe, and rooms of European antiques Ishiya's founder collected — ornate cocoa pots, tin toys, and mechanical music boxes. Kids gravitate toward the miniature garden train that loops around the property and the hands-on cookie-decorating workshop, where visitors can pipe chocolate onto their own biscuit to take home (reservations recommended, as slots are limited). Photographers should budget time outside for the mock-Tudor clock tower and the surrounding rose garden, which is especially photogenic in June and July. Plan on 90 minutes to two hours to cover the museum, workshop, and gift shop without rushing — families with young children often stretch this to three hours given the train ride and outdoor grounds.

The attached café serves baked cheesecake and soft-serve made with the same white chocolate as the signature cookie, and it's worth eating there rather than saving your appetite, since the treats are made fresh on-site rather than shipped in. The gift shop sells factory-fresh Shiroi Koibito tins, often at a modest discount versus airport or department-store pricing, along with park-exclusive flavors and seasonal packaging not sold elsewhere.

## When to go and how long to stay

Weekday mornings shortly after opening are the quietest time, avoiding the tour-bus groups that tend to arrive midday. Winter (December–February) gives the mock-European architecture a snow-dusted, almost Christmas-card look that suits Sapporo's snowy identity, while the rose garden makes early summer the better outdoor season. Either way, this is a half-day stop, not a full-day destination — most visitors fold it into a broader western Sapporo itinerary.

## Nearby & tips

Because Miyanosawa is the Tozai Line's terminus, it's easy to combine the park with a lazy subway ride back through Sapporo's residential neighborhoods rather than backtracking the same way. Bring an appetite for the café before you shop, since post-tour lines at the register can get long when a bus tour is unloading. Photography is generally allowed in the exhibit halls but restricted on the working factory floor itself, so check posted signs as you move through. Confirm current opening hours and admission fees on Ishiya's official site before visiting, as these are adjusted seasonally.
