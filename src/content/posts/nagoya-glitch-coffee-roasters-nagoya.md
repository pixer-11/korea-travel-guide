## Why go

GLITCH COFFEE & ROASTERS built its name in Tokyo's Kanda neighborhood as a rigorously sourced, single-origin specialty coffee shop, and the Nagoya branch in Meieki carries the same philosophy: light-to-medium roasts that highlight the character of a specific farm or lot rather than a house blend designed to taste the same every time. This is the opposite of a chain café — the bean list rotates, the baristas can talk in detail about processing methods (washed, natural, honey) and flavor notes, and the space is built for people who want to slow down over one very good cup rather than grab a to-go latte. For coffee-curious travelers passing through Nagoya Station, it's a legitimate destination in its own right, not just a caffeine pit stop.

## Getting there

The shop sits at 2-chōme-42-2 Meieki, Nakamura Ward — squarely in the Meieki district that fans out from Nagoya Station, one of the largest and busiest terminals in Japan. Nagoya Station is served by JR lines (Tokaido Shinkansen, Tokaido Main Line), the Meitetsu and Kintetsu lines, and the Nagoya Municipal Subway's Higashiyama Line and Sakura-dori Line. From the station's central or Sakura-dori exits, Meieki's grid of office towers and side-street cafés is walkable in under 10 minutes. Because the address is a specific street-level unit rather than inside a mall, it's worth pulling up a map before leaving the station concourse — Meieki's underground passages can be disorienting, and popping up street-level nearby is more reliable than trying to navigate underground the whole way.

## What to try

Expect a short menu built around pour-over (drip) coffee showcasing single origins, plus an espresso-based lineup (espresso, cappuccino, flat white) for those who prefer milk drinks. Staff typically offer a printed or chalkboard list of available beans with origin, farm, and tasting notes — asking "what would you recommend today" is normal and encouraged here, as the lineup changes with what's freshly roasted. Portions are small and prices sit above convenience-store coffee, in line with specialty roasters worldwide; treat it as a considered ten-to-fifteen-minute tasting rather than a large drink to sip for hours. If you're into beans, bags of GLITCH's roasted coffee are usually available to buy and take home — a compact, non-fragile souvenir that's more distinctive than the usual station omiyage.

## When to go

Weekday mornings and early afternoons tend to be calmer, since Meieki's daytime foot traffic is dominated by office workers and Shinkansen transferees rather than tourists. Weekends and the lunch rush can bring a short queue given the shop's compact seating — some counter or standing space rather than large table seating, consistent with GLITCH's original Tokyo format. Because it's inside a station-adjacent business district, expect it to be open through standard café hours but quieter or possibly closed on major holidays; confirm current hours online before a special trip, since these details shift more often than the shop's reputation does.

Meieki rewards a bit of wandering: the district around Nagoya Station is dense with department stores (Takashimaya, Meitetsu), izakaya alleys, and the elevated Sky Promenade observation deck in Midland Square for a skyline view before or after your coffee.
