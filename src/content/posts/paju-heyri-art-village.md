## Why go

Heyri Art Village (헤이리 예술마을) is not an old neighborhood that organically became artsy — it was planned from the ground up in the late 1990s and early 2000s by a collective of writers, artists, architects, and musicians who wanted a car-light, low-rise community built around galleries, studios, and independent bookshops. The result is a sprawling, low-density district of several hundred buildings, many of them striking works of contemporary Korean architecture in concrete, glass, and corten steel, set against the low hills near the Imjin River. It's less about any single blockbuster attraction and more about wandering: unexpected sculpture gardens, small private museums devoted to a single artist or theme, cafés inside architect-designed houses, and quiet lanes that feel far removed from Seoul's density despite being under an hour away. It's a favorite half-day escape for design-minded travelers and families looking for something calmer than the city center.

## Getting there

Heyri sits in Tanhyeon-myeon, Paju, roughly 30–35 km northwest of Seoul, along the same corridor that leads toward Imjingak and the DMZ border area. There is no subway station directly in the village, so most visitors combine two legs:

- **Bus + subway combo:** Take Seoul Subway Line 3 to Daegok Station, then transfer to a local bus (the 021, 058, or similar routes serving Tanhyeon/Heyri) — travel time from Daegok is about 20–30 minutes depending on traffic.
- **Direct bus from Seoul:** Intercity or express buses run from Hapjeong or Sinchon areas toward Paju's Tanhyeon/Heyri stop; check current schedules since routes are periodically adjusted.
- **Car or taxi:** Driving takes about 40–50 minutes from central Seoul via the Jayuro (Freedom Road), and Heyri has ample parking, which makes a rental car or taxi the least fussy option for families.

Many visitors also book a half-day tour that bundles Heyri with Imjingak or the Third Tunnel, since they sit along the same route north.

## What to see and do

Budget at least 3–4 hours to walk the village properly; it's larger and hillier than photos suggest. Highlights to look for as you wander:

- **Small themed museums** — Heyri is known for niche collections: rooms devoted to book art, photography, illustration, and Korean modern art. Names and hours change, so treat this as a "discover as you go" experience rather than following a fixed checklist.
- **Architecture itself** — Many buildings were designed by notable Korean architects as personal statements, so even skipping the paid exhibits, the walk past corrugated steel facades, exposed concrete, and cantilevered rooflines is worthwhile.
- **Bookshops and design stores** — Independent bookshops and craft/design boutiques are scattered throughout, reflecting the founders' literary roots.
- **Cafés with a view** — Several multi-story cafés built into the hillside offer rooftop or window seating overlooking the village rooftops and surrounding countryside — a good rest stop midway through a walk.
- **Outdoor sculpture and open lawns** — Public art installations sit between buildings, and open green spaces make it comfortable for kids to run around between stops.

## When to go

Spring (April–May) and autumn (late September–November) are the most comfortable seasons, with mild temperatures for walking outdoors between buildings. Weekday mornings are noticeably quieter than weekends, when Seoul day-trippers and school groups arrive in larger numbers. Winter visits are workable but several outdoor spaces feel bare, and some smaller independent galleries reduce hours or close for the season — confirm before making a special trip in January or February.

## Nearby & tips

Heyri is close enough to **Paju Book City**, a separate planned district of publishing houses and bookshops, to combine both in one day. It's also on the way to **Imjingak Park** and the broader DMZ tour circuit, so many itineraries pair a relaxed Heyri morning with a DMZ-area afternoon. Wear shoes suited to hills and uneven paving, since the village has real elevation changes between clusters of buildings, and note that not every museum accepts cash-only or card-only — carrying both remains sensible for smaller vendors.
