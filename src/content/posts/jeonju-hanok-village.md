## What to know

Jeonju Hanok Village is one of Korea's most visited traditional neighborhoods, made up of hundreds of hanok (traditional wooden houses with curved tiled roofs) clustered into a walkable historic district. Unlike a museum, it's a living neighborhood — some hanok are private homes, others have been converted into guesthouses, cafes, craft workshops, and small museums. The village is closely tied to Jeonju's identity as a food city, and the area is especially known as a place to try regional specialties, so wandering the food streets is as much a part of the experience as looking at the architecture itself.

Expect a mix of quiet residential lanes and much busier main streets lined with snack stalls, hanbok rental shops, and souvenir stores. On weekends and holidays, the main strips can get very crowded, with long lines at popular food stalls and heavy foot traffic. Weekday mornings offer a noticeably calmer experience, especially before mid-morning tour groups arrive.

Renting a hanbok (traditional Korean dress) for a few hours is one of the most popular things to do here — it's common to see visitors in colorful hanbok posing against the tiled rooftops. Many of the traditional buildings and some cultural sites in the area offer free or discounted admission to visitors wearing hanbok, so if you plan to visit any indoor cultural sites, renting one first can be worthwhile.

## How to get there

Jeonju is reachable from Seoul by train (to Jeonju Station) or intercity/express bus (to Jeonju Bus Terminal), with the journey typically taking a couple of hours depending on the service. From either the train station or bus terminal, the Hanok Village is a short taxi ride or a bus connection away — it is not within easy walking distance of the station itself, so plan on a short additional trip once you arrive in the city.

Once inside the village, everything is walkable, and this is really a neighborhood best explored on foot. Comfortable, flat shoes are strongly recommended, since the streets are a mix of pavement and traditional stone paths, and you'll likely be walking for several hours if you explore fully, especially if you're doing so in a hanbok.

## Tips

- Visit early in the day or on a weekday to avoid the heaviest crowds, particularly if you want unobstructed photos of the rooftop streetscapes.
- Carry some cash, as smaller food stalls and traditional shops may not all accept cards.
- Wear comfortable shoes — the village is large and best experienced by wandering rather than following a fixed route.
- If you rent a hanbok, ask the shop about which nearby attractions offer free or discounted entry for hanbok wearers.
- Leave time to simply get a little lost in the quieter side alleys away from the main food street, where much of the traditional residential character of the village is best preserved.
- Pace yourself with food — the area is known for encouraging visitors to graze across multiple stalls rather than eat one big meal, so sampling smaller portions from several places is a common approach.
