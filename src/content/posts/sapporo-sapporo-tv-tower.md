## Why go

Sapporo TV Tower has anchored the skyline at the east end of Odori Park since the late 1950s, making it one of the city's most recognizable landmarks alongside the Sapporo Clock Tower. It's not the tallest or newest observation tower in Japan, and it won't pretend to be — but that's part of its appeal. The red steel lattice structure has a genuinely retro, mid-century charm, and the observation deck delivers a clean, unobstructed view straight down Odori Park's tree-lined promenade toward Mt. Moiwa and the Teine mountain range on clear days. With a 4.1 rating from nearly 17,000 reviews, it's a well-worn but reliably satisfying stop, especially for first-time visitors trying to get their bearings on Sapporo's grid layout.

## Getting there

The tower sits at 1 Chome Odorinishi, Chuo Ward, right at the eastern tip of Odori Park, which makes it one of the easiest attractions in Sapporo to reach on foot. The closest subway stop is Odori Station, served by the Namboku, Tozai, and Toho lines — from there it's roughly a 5-minute walk east through the park to the tower's base. Sapporo Station is about a 15-minute walk north, or one stop away by subway if you'd rather not walk. Because the tower marks the literal end of Odori Park, it's a natural point to orient yourself before exploring the rest of central Sapporo, including the Sapporo Clock Tower a few blocks northwest.

## What to see and do

The main draw is the observation deck, set at around 90 meters above street level, reached by elevator in under a minute. From the top, the view runs the length of Odori Park's flowerbeds and fountains toward the Sapporo Municipal Government building and the mountains beyond — during the Sapporo Snow Festival (early February), this is one of the best vantage points to see the park's giant snow sculptures laid out in a row. On clear evenings, the deck is popular for sunset and city-light views, with the tower itself lit up at night, giving it a different personality after dark than during a daytime visit.

Below the observation floor, the lower levels have small souvenir shops and a café area, plus occasional seasonal exhibits or photo-op installations. A full visit — elevator up, a loop around the deck, elevator down — typically takes 30 to 45 minutes, making it an easy add-on rather than a half-day commitment. Because operating hours and admission fees are adjusted periodically, confirm current pricing and closing times on the official Sapporo TV Tower website or at the ticket counter before planning around it.

## When to go

Early evening, roughly an hour before sunset, tends to reward visitors with both daylight panoramas and the transition into the city's lit-up night view — good for photography without needing to time a second visit. Winter (December to February) offers a dramatic, snow-covered version of Odori Park below, especially during the Snow Festival, though it's also Sapporo's coldest and busiest tourist stretch. Summer months bring the Yosakoi Soran Festival and Sapporo Summer Festival's beer gardens, both staged in Odori Park directly beneath the tower, so a same-visit combination is easy to arrange from June through August.

## Nearby & tips

Odori Park itself deserves at least 20–30 minutes of a stroll, whether before or after the tower — it's Sapporo's central green spine, dividing the city into east and west blocks and lined with fountains, sculptures, and seasonal flower displays. For a higher and more expansive view of the whole city, including the tower itself as a small landmark below, the Mt. Moiwa Ropeway on the city's southern edge is the classic follow-up, though it requires a short bus or taxi ride. The Sapporo Clock Tower and Former Hokkaido Government Office Building are both within a 10–15 minute walk northwest, making a loop through downtown's historic core an efficient half-day itinerary anchored by the TV Tower.
