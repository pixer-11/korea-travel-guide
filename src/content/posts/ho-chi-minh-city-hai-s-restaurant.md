## Why Hai's Restaurant Stands Out

With a 4.9 rating across nearly 8,000 reviews, Hai's Restaurant is one of the most consistently praised dining spots in Ho Chi Minh City for visitors wanting reliable, well-executed Vietnamese food without gambling on a random street stall. It's built its reputation over years of serving tourists and long-term expats alike, which shows in the polish: English-speaking staff, a menu that explains dishes clearly, and a dining room that's comfortable for those easing into Vietnamese cuisine for the first time. It's not a hidden gem — it's a dependable institution, and that's exactly its appeal.

## Getting There

The restaurant sits at 257 Lý Tự Trọng, in Bến Thành ward, District 1 — the historic heart of the city. It's roughly a 10-minute walk from Ben Thanh Market, making it easy to combine with a market visit, especially in the early evening once the day market winds down and the night stalls set up outside. From the Bitexco Financial Tower or Nguyen Hue Walking Street, it's about a 15–20 minute walk, or a five-minute taxi/Grab ride. Lý Tự Trọng runs parallel to Le Loi Boulevard, so if you're staying anywhere near the Opera House, Notre-Dame Cathedral, or the Reunification Palace, you're within easy reach on foot or by cyclo. Grab (the regional ride-hailing app) is the simplest way to get an exact drop-off, since street-level signage in this dense commercial block can be easy to miss.

## What to Eat

Expect a broad, crowd-pleasing spread of Vietnamese staples rather than a narrow specialty menu. Typical standouts reviewers highlight include fresh and fried spring rolls (gỏi cuốn and chả giò), pho, claypot caramelized fish or pork (cá kho tộ), grilled seafood, and stir-fried morning glory (rau muống xào tỏi) — solid anchors for a first-timer's tasting tour of the cuisine. Portions are generally sized for sharing, so a group of three or four can order five or six dishes family-style and sample widely. Because pricing and menu items shift over time, treat any specific price you see online as a rough guide and confirm current offerings and cost when you sit down.

## When to Go

Ho Chi Minh City's dining scene runs on early evenings — arriving between 6:00 and 7:00pm gets you seated quickly and lets you watch the restaurant fill up rather than wait in it. Weekends and holiday periods (Tet in particular, typically late January to mid-February) bring heavier crowds and possible shortened or altered hours, so it's worth a quick call or check of current hours before heading over. Weekday lunch is another good window if you prefer a quieter room. Ho Chi Minh City's climate is tropical, with a dry season roughly November through April that's generally the most comfortable time to be walking between District 1 sights before or after your meal; the wet season (May–October) brings short, heavy afternoon downpours, so an evening reservation is a safer bet than a midday one.

## Nearby & Practical Tips

Pair a visit with a stroll through Ben Thanh Market for souvenirs and street snacks, or extend the evening at the Bui Vien / Pham Ngu Lao backpacker strip a bit further south for bars and live music. The Saigon Central Post Office and Notre-Dame Cathedral are both within a 20-minute walk if you want to sightsee beforehand and arrive hungry. Given the restaurant's popularity, larger groups should reserve ahead, especially on weekend evenings — with nearly 8,000 reviews and a 4.9 average, tables can turn over quickly but demand is high. Cash and cards are both commonly accepted at established District 1 restaurants, but carrying some Vietnamese dong is still wise in case of card outages or small surcharges.
