## Why go

Kampong Glam is one of Singapore's four historic ethnic districts, set aside by Stamford Raffles' 1822 town plan as the quarter for the Malay, Arab, and Bugis communities. Its centerpiece is Masjid Sultan, a golden-domed mosque completed in 1928 (replacing an earlier 1824 structure) that remains an active place of worship and the visual anchor of the whole neighborhood. Unlike the polished, ticketed heritage sites elsewhere in the city, this district is a living one — shophouses that once housed textile traders and religious goods stores now mix with independent boutiques, kopi joints, and mural-covered lanes, while the call to prayer still marks the rhythm of the day. At 26 Bussorah St, the address most associated with the area's charm, you're standing on a pedestrianized street that frames the mosque perfectly and is lined with restored two-story shophouses in soft pastel tones.

## Getting there

The closest station is Bugis MRT (East-West Line and Downtown Line), roughly a 7-10 minute walk via Victoria Street or North Bridge Road. Exit toward Bugis Junction/Fu Lu Shou Complex and head northeast; you'll know you're close when North Bridge Road starts showing Arabic-script shopfront signs. Nicoll Highway MRT (Circle Line) is a slightly longer alternative walk from the east side. If arriving by taxi or ride-hail, ask for Bussorah Street or Arab Street directly, since the whole quarter is a pedestrian-friendly grid best explored on foot once you arrive — cars are restricted on parts of Bussorah Street itself.

## What to see and do

Start at **Masjid Sultan**, free to enter outside prayer times (modest dress and a robe loan are typically available at the entrance for visitors). Walk down **Bussorah Street** for the postcard view back toward the mosque's dome, then detour into **Arab Street** for shops selling batik, songket textiles, baskets, and perfume oils sold by weight — a holdover from the district's original trading identity. **Haji Lane**, one street over, is the district's narrow, mural-lined strip of independent fashion boutiques and small cafes, best appreciated slowly on foot since it's barely wide enough for two people to pass. The **Malay Heritage Centre**, housed in the former Istana Kampong Glam (the old royal palace), covers the area's role as seat of the Johor-Riau Malay royalty before Raffles' arrival. Budget 2-3 hours for a relaxed loop of all four; add 45 minutes if you want to sit down for kopi or teh tarik along the way.

## What to eat

The district is a reliable spot for Middle Eastern and Malay food side by side: look for nasi padang stalls, murtabak (stuffed savory pancake) counters along North Bridge Road, and shisha cafes that spill onto the sidewalk after dark near Arab Street. Zam Zam and Victory restaurants (both long-running North Bridge Road institutions) are known for murtabak and biryani if you want a sit-down meal rather than a snack crawl.

## When to go

Weekday mornings (9-11am) are quietest, before tour groups and lunch crowds arrive; the mosque and shops are open and unhurried. Late afternoon into evening brings a livelier, more social atmosphere as cafes fill and shisha lounges light up, which suits visitors wanting atmosphere over calm. Friday midday is the one time to plan around respectfully, since it's the main congregational prayer time at Masjid Sultan and the area around the mosque gets busy with worshippers.

## How to visit like a local

Because Kampong Glam is still relatively under-the-radar compared to Chinatown or Little India, an early or late-afternoon weekday visit lets you enjoy it without feeling rushed. Dress modestly if you plan to enter Masjid Sultan — shoulders and knees covered — and remove shoes before stepping onto carpeted prayer areas; robes are usually available near the entrance for those who need them. Most shops and cafes accept cards or mobile payment (PayNow is standard locally), but carrying some small cash is useful for the smaller textile and spice stalls. Tipping isn't customary in Singapore, including here. The most common visitor mistake is treating Haji Lane and Bussorah Street as the whole district and skipping the surrounding blocks — wander one street further out for quieter shopfronts and better prices on textiles and perfume oils.
