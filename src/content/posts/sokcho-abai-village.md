## What Abai Village is

Abai Village is a small peninsula neighborhood in Sokcho, on South Korea's northeastern coast, separated from the rest of the city by a narrow channel connecting the harbor to the sea. It's known primarily as a community founded by refugees who fled from Hamgyong province in North Korea during the Korean War and settled here, expecting to return home once the fighting ended. That return never happened, and the village grew into a lasting community that has preserved elements of North Korean-style food and daily life for decades. "Abai" itself is a Hamgyong-dialect term for an older man, reflecting the community's origins.

Today the area is a low-key, walkable pocket of Sokcho that draws visitors curious about this layer of Korean history, along with anyone looking for a distinctive coastal food experience and a quieter alternative to the busier tourist strips near Sokcho's beaches and Seoraksan-bound bus terminals.

## How to get there

The signature way to reach Abai Village is the gaetbae, a small ferry that residents pull across the narrow channel by hand using an overhead cable, rather than a motor. It's a short, inexpensive crossing and doubles as a genuine local-life experience rather than a tourist gimmick — it's still an everyday commuter route for people living in the village. Alternatively, you can walk or drive around via the harbor bridge that connects the peninsula to central Sokcho, which is useful if you want to explore at your own pace or arrive with luggage.

Sokcho itself is most commonly reached from Seoul or other regions by intercity or express bus, as there's no direct KTX line into the city center; from the bus terminal, taxis or local buses can get you to the harbor area near the ferry crossing.

## What to expect and do

- **Wander the village streets.** Abai Village is compact and easy to cover on foot, with narrow lanes, modest homes, and a fishing-community atmosphere quite different from downtown Sokcho.
- **Try the local food.** The area is particularly associated with squid-based dishes reflecting both the region's fishing industry and the culinary traditions refugees brought with them. Sundae (Korean blood sausage) made with squid instead of the usual casing is a specialty tied to this neighborhood's history.
- **Take in the harbor views.** The channel crossing and waterfront give good views of Sokcho's fishing boats and the working harbor.

## Tips

- Bring small cash for the ferry crossing, as this is a simple, informal local operation.
- Pair a visit with nearby Sokcho attractions like the beach or harbor markets, since the village is close to the city center.
- Visit during the day when the ferry and village are most active; it's a quiet residential area, so keep noise considerate for residents.
- This is a lived-in neighborhood, not a museum recreation — treat it as such and be respectful when taking photos of homes and daily life.
