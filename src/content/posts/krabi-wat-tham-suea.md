## Why go

Wat Tham Suea, or "Tiger Cave Temple," is one of southern Thailand's most atmospheric forest monasteries, built into a limestone cliff surrounded by dense jungle a short ride from Krabi Town. It draws pilgrims and travelers for two reasons: an extensive network of caves and shrines set into the rock at ground level, and a punishing but rewarding staircase — commonly cited at 1,237 steps — that climbs to a hilltop chedi and a large gold Buddha statue with sweeping views over Krabi's karst hills and, on clear days, the Andaman Sea in the distance. With a 4.6 rating across nearly 14,000 reviews, it's firmly established as one of the region's must-see attractions, but it rewards visitors who come prepared for a genuine hike rather than a casual temple stroll.

## Getting there

The temple is located at 35 Tambon Krabi Noi, Amphoe Mueang Krabi, roughly 8km inland from central Krabi Town. There's no direct public bus route, so most visitors arrive by rented scooter (about a 15-20 minute ride from town), metered taxi, or a tuk-tuk/songthaew arranged through a guesthouse — round-trip tuk-tuk fares are usually negotiated on the spot, so agree on a price and a pickup time before you set off, since taxis rarely wait around at the summit. Some Krabi tour operators also include Wat Tham Suea as a stop on half-day countryside tours alongside the Krabi Town clock tower and Emerald/Blue Pool at Khao Nor Chuchi. If self-driving, follow signs for "Tiger Cave Temple" off the Krabi–Ao Luek road; parking near the base is informal but plentiful.

## What to see

Start at ground level, where the working monastery complex includes a large ordination hall, several cave shrines cut into the cliff face, and grounds shaded by rubber and rain trees — monks in residence make this an active place of worship, not just a tourist site. Look for the cave that gives the temple its name, where a tiger's paw print is said to have been found, along with statues and reliefs tucked into rock crevices throughout the forest paths.

The main draw, though, is the staircase to the summit. The climb is steep, uneven in places, and largely unshaded near the top, but the payoff is a golden seated Buddha and a small chedi perched on the cliff edge with a 360-degree view of Krabi's jungle and karst topography — a genuinely striking sight and one of the better panoramic viewpoints in the province. Expect the ascent to take 45-90 minutes depending on fitness and how often you stop; the descent is faster but hard on the knees, so trekking poles or a slow, careful pace help.

## When to go

Aim to arrive as close to opening as possible — the temple grounds typically open around 5:00-6:00am — to climb before the mid-morning heat sets in and before tour groups arrive. Early morning also brings the best light for photos from the summit. The dry season, roughly November through March, offers the clearest skies and least slippery steps; during the rainy season (June to October) the stairs can be wet and the view obscured by cloud. Avoid climbing in the midday sun regardless of season; there's minimal shade on the upper stretches and heat exhaustion is a real risk.

## Nearby & tips

Cover shoulders and knees out of respect for the working temple, and remove shoes before entering shrine buildings. Bring water — there's little to no vendor access once you start climbing — and wear proper shoes rather than sandals, since the steps are uneven concrete and can be slick with moisture or moss. Macaques live along the route and around the base; keep food out of sight and don't offer it to them. Combine the visit with other Krabi countryside stops like Khao Khanab Nam's twin limestone peaks or the Emerald Pool and Hot Springs near Khao Nor Chuchi Forest Park to make a fuller half-day trip out of the trek.
