## Why Hahoe Village Matters

Hahoe ("river turns around the village") sits on a peninsula formed by a bend in the Nakdong River, which explains both its name and its historic isolation. Founded and shaped over 600 years by the Ryu clan of Pungsan, it's one of Korea's best-preserved yangban (aristocratic) villages, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2010 alongside Yangdong Village in Gyeongju under the "Historic Villages of Korea" listing. Unlike restored folk villages built for tourism, Hahoe is a living village — families still occupy many of the tile-roofed and thatch-roofed houses, and the layout of stone walls, courtyards, and pine groves reflects genuine Joseon-era social hierarchy, with grander tiled homes near the center and humbler thatched houses toward the edges.

## Getting There

From Andong Intercity Bus Terminal or Andong Station, city bus 46 runs directly to Hahoe Village roughly every 30-40 minutes, with the ride taking about 50 minutes. A taxi from central Andong takes 25-30 minutes and costs a fixed amount that's easy to confirm with the driver beforehand. If arriving from Seoul, the KTX to Andong Station followed by the local bus is the simplest combination; there's no direct train to the village itself. Drivers can park at the visitor lot near the entrance, from which a free or low-cost shuttle typically ferries visitors the last stretch to the village gate — private cars are not allowed to drive directly into the historic core.

## What to See and Do

Start at the Hahoe Mask Museum near the entrance, home to the famous Hahoe Talnori masks — wooden grotesque-comic faces (Yangban, Seonbi, Gaksi, Chorango, and others) used in the centuries-old Hahoe Byeolsingut Talnori mask dance, itself a UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage element. Inside the village, look for Yangjindang and Chunghyodang, two of the largest and most architecturally significant houses, both designated Korean Treasures, and Byeongsan Confucian Academy's sister site connections nearby (Byeongsan Seowon itself sits a short drive away and is worth combining if you have a full day).

The signature natural feature is Buyongdae, a sheer cliff face on the opposite bank draped in pine trees, best reached by a small ferry that crosses the river from the village (seasonal, weather-dependent) or by a longer walking/driving route around. The view from the top back across the river bend toward the village rooftops is the classic Hahoe photograph. Along the riverbank near the village, a grove of centuries-old pine trees called Manson Jeong (Pine Forest) offers shade and a quieter walk.

If your visit coincides with a scheduled performance, the Hahoe Mask Dance is staged at a dedicated outdoor theater near the village entrance, usually on a limited weekly schedule (often weekends) — check the current calendar locally since performances aren't daily.

## When to Go and How Long to Stay

Spring (April-May) and autumn (October-early November) bring the best weather and rice-paddy or foliage color around the village. Summer is humid and the ferry crossing can be canceled after heavy rain. Winter is quiet and atmospheric with far fewer crowds, though some facilities scale back. Plan for 3-4 hours to walk the village, visit the mask museum, and cross to Buyongdae; add another hour if you catch a mask dance performance.

## Nearby & Practical Tips

Andong's other major site, the Dosan Seowon Confucian academy, sits about 30-40 minutes northeast and pairs well with a full-day itinerary built around Confucian heritage. Back in Andong city, sample the local specialty jjimdak (soy-braised chicken with noodles and vegetables) at the well-known Andong Jjimdak Street near the old downtown, and Andong-style soju, a traditional distilled spirit with its own dedicated small museum. Wear shoes suited to dirt paths and gravel, bring cash for the ferry crossing and small vendor stalls, and confirm current admission fees and shuttle/ferry operating hours before you go, as these are adjusted seasonally.
