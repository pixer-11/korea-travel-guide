## Why go

Monza is the fastest, most historic circuit still on the F1 calendar — a "Temple of Speed" built in 1922 inside a former royal park, where cars top 350 km/h down the long straights before the Parabolica corner. Ferrari's home race turns the grandstands red every year; when Ferrari wins here, the podium ceremony is followed by tens of thousands of fans invading the track, a scene unique to Monza. The 2026 edition marks the 97th running of the Italian Grand Prix and the 15th round of that season's championship, so title-fight stakes are usually still live by early September.

## Getting there

The circuit sits inside Parco di Monza, about 15 km northeast of central Milan. Most visitors base themselves in Milan and travel out on race weekend:

- **Train + shuttle**: Take a regional train from Milano Centrale or Milano Cadorna to Monza station (roughly 15-20 minutes), then catch one of the dedicated shuttle buses that run from the town center to the circuit's public entrances on race days.
- **Direct shuttle buses**: On event weekends, extra bus services typically run straight from Milan (Lampugnano and other hubs) to Monza Park — check the official ACI/Monza circuit site closer to the date for routes and pickup points.
- **Driving**: Parking near the autodromo fills up fast and road closures are extensive; if self-driving, arrive early morning and expect a long walk from remote lots.

Flying in, Milan Malpensa (MXP) and Milan Linate (LIN) are the main international gateways; Bergamo Orio al Serio (BGY) is a budget-carrier alternative, each roughly 40-60 minutes from Monza depending on traffic.

## What to see and do

- **Friday practice**: Quieter, cheaper grounds-access days are a good chance to walk the paddock area, watch cars at low-key sessions, and scout your grandstand sightlines for Sunday.
- **Saturday qualifying**: Sets the grid order and is often nearly as tense as the race itself, especially in Monza's slipstream-heavy layout where a single lap can be decided by tenths.
- **Sunday race day**: Arrive well before lights-out — security lines and foot traffic from the station/shuttle drop-off to the grandstands can take over an hour on race morning.
- **Parco di Monza**: Beyond the track, the circuit is surrounded by parkland, the Villa Reale di Monza (a Neoclassical royal palace), and old banked oval sections of the original 1922 track, still visible as a piece of motorsport history worth a walking detour.
- **Monza town center**: The Duomo di Monza, home to the Iron Crown (Corona Ferrea) used in medieval royal coronations, is a short walk from the train station and an easy add-on before or after the race.

## When to go and how long to stay

Early September in Lombardy is usually warm and dry, ideal for a full day in open grandstands — bring sun protection and water regardless of forecast. Plan for the full Friday-to-Sunday arc if you want practice and qualifying too; a single-day Sunday visit works if you only care about the race, but expect the biggest crowds and highest ticket demand for race day. Many fans build in a Milan city stay either side of the weekend, since the metro area offers far more lodging and flight options than Monza town itself.

## Tickets and practical tips

Grandstand seats (e.g., Ascari, Parabolica, Main Straight) and General Admission "prato" (lawn) areas sell out well ahead of the weekend, sometimes months in advance, so buy directly through the official Italian Grand Prix or Formula1.com ticket channels rather than resellers. Public transport can get congested both to and from the circuit after the race ends — allow extra time for the return trip and consider staying in Monza itself on Sunday night if flights are early Monday.

**Always verify the final schedule, ticket prices, and any route changes on the official Formula 1 and Autodromo Nazionale Monza websites before booking**, since specifics can shift as the event approaches.
