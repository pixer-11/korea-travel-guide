## Why go

Telok Ayer Street is one of the best-preserved stretches of shophouse architecture in Chinatown, and My Awesome Cafe occupies one of these restored units, mixing exposed brick, salvaged furniture, and warm lighting into a space that feels more like a friend's well-decorated living room than a formal restaurant. The menu leans into handcrafted salads and sandwiches with a rotating wine and spirits list, which explains why it draws both the laptop-and-latte weekday crowd and a livelier dinner-and-drinks set on weekends. With a 4.7 rating across nearly 7,900 reviews, it's firmly one of the neighborhood's most talked-about cafes rather than a quiet local secret — plan around the crowds rather than expecting to beat them.

## Getting there

The cafe sits at 202 Telok Ayer St, Singapore 068639, in the heart of Chinatown's Telok Ayer conservation district. The closest MRT station is Telok Ayer (Downlow Line), roughly a 5-minute walk — take Exit A and head north along Telok Ayer Street, passing Thian Hock Keng Temple on your left. Alternatively, Tanjong Pagar MRT (East-West Line) is about a 10-minute walk from the opposite direction via Amoy Street. Both routes are flat, shaded by shophouse facades for parts of the way, and walkable in normal daytime heat. Street parking along Telok Ayer and Amoy is metered and limited, so the train is the more reliable option, especially at lunch when the area is busy with office workers.

## What to see and eat

Expect a menu built around composed salads and open-faced or stacked sandwiches, the kind of food that photographs well and travels light for a midday meal — a good fit for the area's mix of finance workers and design-district visitors. The wine and spirits list rounds things out for an evening visit rather than a quick coffee stop. Because it's categorized as a full restaurant rather than a grab-and-go cafe, plan for a sit-down meal of 45 minutes to just over an hour rather than a fast pit stop. The interior's repurposed decor — think reclaimed wood tables, vintage signage, and mismatched but curated seating — is worth a slow look, and the shophouse setting means seating is limited, so larger groups may need extra patience.

## When to go

Weekday late mornings (around 11am, just before the lunch rush) or the gap between lunch and dinner (roughly 2:30–5pm) tend to be calmer given the venue's popularity. Weekends bring brunch crowds and, later in the day, a bar-and-drinks atmosphere that shifts the mood from cafe to lounge. Singapore's tropical climate means the streets outside are hot and humid most of the year, so an air-conditioned indoor table is a reasonable respite regardless of season; just note that outdoor shophouse seating, if offered, will be warmer during midday hours.

## How to visit like a local

Given the venue's very-popular status, the smartest move is timing, not secrecy — arrive right at opening or in that early-afternoon lull to avoid a queue, since peak lunch and weekend dinner slots routinely fill up given the review volume. If a queue does form, it's standard in Singapore to add your name and wait rather than hover near occupied tables. Payment is almost always by card or mobile pay (PayNow, Grab Pay, and contactless cards are widely accepted across Singapore cafes), and tipping is not customary — a service charge, if any, is usually already built into the bill, so there's no need to leave extra cash. Order at your table once seated rather than at a counter, and if you want a specific salad or sandwich that's proven popular based on the review count, asking staff which dish is the current favorite is a normal and welcomed question. The most common visitor mistake is treating this as a quick coffee stop and rushing in without a table wait buffer — build in 15–20 minutes of flexibility, especially on Friday and Saturday evenings. Afterward, use the location to explore on foot: Thian Hock Keng Temple, Nagore Dargah, and the Amoy Street hawker stalls are all within a few minutes' walk, making this a natural anchor for a half-day Chinatown-and-CBD wander rather than a standalone destination.
