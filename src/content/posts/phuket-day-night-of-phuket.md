## Why go

Old Town Phuket isn't short on restaurants chasing Instagram likes with mediocre food, so a spot holding a 4.7 rating across more than 4,700 reviews is worth noting. Day & Night Of Phuket, tucked into the Talat Yai area of Mueang Phuket District, has built its reputation the old-fashioned way: consistent Thai cooking, generous portions, and a menu that doesn't feel dumbed down for tourists. The name suggests its appeal — it works equally well as a midday refuel spot after wandering Thalang Road's heritage buildings, or as an evening base before you head toward Phuket Walking Street (Lard Yai), which runs Sunday evenings along the same historic core.

## Getting there

Talat Yai sits in the heart of Phuket Old Town, the district famous for Sino-Portuguese architecture, shuttered shophouses turned cafes, and murals tucked into side alleys. There's no rail system in Phuket, so most visitors arrive by rented scooter, Grab, or metered taxi. If you're coming from Patong or the west coast beaches, expect roughly a 30–45 minute drive depending on traffic on Route 4020/Thepkrasattri Road. From Phuket International Airport, budget closer to an hour. Parking in Old Town is tight on weekends, especially near Thalang Road and Soi Rommani, so arriving by Grab or taxi and walking the last stretch is usually less stressful than hunting for a spot.

## What to eat

Given the price level (moderate, roughly the same bracket as a mid-range Thai restaurant rather than a street stall or fine-dining venue), expect a menu built around southern Thai flavors and Phuket-style seafood — think dishes leaning on turmeric, cashew nuts, and the sour-spicy profile the region is known for. Phuket's culinary identity blends Thai, Chinese Hokkien, and Malay-Peranakan influences, a legacy of the island's tin-mining and trading past, so look for the crossover dishes on the menu: Hokkien-style stir-fried noodles, curries with a coconut base, and fresh seafood prepared simply with garlic, chili, or lime. Because reviewer volume is this high, portions and consistency are clearly part of the draw — this isn't a place serving three tables a night, so kitchen turnover tends to keep dishes fresh.

## When to go

"Day & Night" in the name is a fair description of how to use it — the restaurant suits both lunch and dinner crowds. For lunch, aim for 12:00–1:30 pm before the midday heat peaks, then retreat into an air-conditioned café or museum (the Thavorn Hotel Museum and Phuket Thai Hua Museum are both a short walk into Old Town). For dinner, 5:30–6:30 pm beats the 7:30–9 pm rush that hits most Old Town restaurants once the day-trip crowds return from the beaches. If your visit lands on a Sunday, timing dinner before Lard Yai Walking Street opens (usually late afternoon into evening) lets you eat first, then browse street food stalls and local crafts without doing both on an empty or overly full stomach. November through February brings Phuket's coolest, driest weather and the most comfortable conditions for walking Old Town afterward; April through October is hotter and wetter, so an early or late dining slot matters more.

## Nearby & tips

Talat Yai's grid of streets rewards a slow walk — Old Town's photogenic corners cluster around Thalang Road, Dibuk Road, and Soi Rommani, all within 10–15 minutes on foot. Pair a meal here with a stop at Chinpracha House or the Phuket Old Town street art trail, both established fixtures of the district. Reservations aren't typically required at this price and style of restaurant, but given the review volume, arriving right at opening for your chosen meal service reduces wait times. As with most independent restaurants in Thailand, confirm current opening hours and any menu price specifics locally or via their listed contact, since these details shift more often than the food itself.
