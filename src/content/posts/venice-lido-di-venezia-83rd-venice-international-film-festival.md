## What the festival is

The Venice International Film Festival — La Biennale di Venezia's Mostra Internazionale d'Arte Cinematografica — is the oldest film festival in the world, founded in 1932. The 83rd edition is scheduled for September 2–12, 2026, and it remains one of the "Big Three" alongside Cannes and Berlin, functioning as an early launchpad for awards-season contenders (Golden Lion for best film) as well as a magnet for A-list premieres. Maggie Gyllenhaal will serve as Jury President for this edition, continuing the festival's tradition of naming a prominent filmmaker or actor to lead the jury each year.

Unlike Cannes, Venice happens almost entirely on the Lido di Venezia, the barrier island east of the historic center, which gives the whole event a distinct, walkable, beach-town character rather than a dense urban festival footprint.

## Where it happens

The hub is the **Palazzo del Cinema** on Lungomare Guglielmo Marconi, the Lido's main seafront boulevard, alongside the adjacent **Palazzo del Casinò** and the **PalaBiennale** tent structures that host additional screening rooms, the press area, and industry events. Red carpet arrivals typically take place on the Palazzo del Cinema steps facing the Marconi promenade — this stretch is the best free vantage point for star-spotting, though space fills early and access can be restricted during peak arrivals.

## Getting there

Fly into Venice Marco Polo Airport (VCE), then reach the Lido via the standard Venice route: airport bus or Alilaguna waterbus to Venice proper (Piazzale Roma or San Marco), followed by a **vaporetto** (public waterbus) directly to Lido station — lines 1, 5.1, 5.2, and 6 all call at the Lido depending on route and season. From the Lido vaporetto stop, it's roughly a 10–15 minute walk or a short bus ride (line A or B) along Gran Viale Santa Maria Elisabetta to Lungomare Marconi and the festival venues. There is no bridge or train link to the Lido — everything arrives by boat, so build in extra time, especially with festival crowds converging on the same vaporetto lines.

## Attending as a non-industry visitor

Most gala premieres are reserved for accreditation holders, guests, and journalists, but the festival typically opens a portion of tickets to the general public through an official online booking system in the weeks before opening night — this is the channel to watch, not scalpers or third-party resellers. Public tickets, when available, are usually for daytime and repeat screenings rather than the evening red-carpet galas. Confirm the specific procedure, dates, and prices on the official La Biennale website (labiennale.org) once they're published, since these details change yearly and were not finalized at the time of writing.

Beyond ticketed screenings, the festival atmosphere itself — outdoor giant screens, photo calls, and the general buzz along Lungomare Marconi — is free to experience simply by being on the Lido during the ten days.

## When to go and practical tips

- **Book Lido accommodation as early as possible.** Rooms on the island itself (rather than commuting from Venice proper) are limited and sell out fast once dates are confirmed.
- **Arrive at screening/red-carpet spots well ahead of scheduled times**; festival crowds combined with narrow promenade space mean good viewing spots go quickly.
- **Pack for early September heat** — the Lido is exposed and sunny, with limited shade near the Palazzo del Cinema.
- **Combine with Venice proper**: the historic center is a short vaporetto ride away, so a festival trip pairs naturally with a few days exploring St. Mark's Square, the Rialto, or Murano and Burano.
- **Check the official schedule regularly**, since screening times, ticket windows, and any program changes are only confirmed close to opening.
