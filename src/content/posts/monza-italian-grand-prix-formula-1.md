## Why go

Monza is Formula 1's cathedral of speed — the fastest circuit on the calendar and, alongside Silverstone, one of only two tracks that have hosted a round of the world championship every year since 1950 (bar 1980). The Autodromo Nazionale Monza sits inside the Parco di Monza, a former royal hunting ground, so the paddock, grandstands, and famous Curva Grande and Parabolica corners are wrapped in centuries-old parkland rather than a purpose-built industrial park. The Tifosi turn this into a de facto Ferrari home race, and when a red car wins, the podium and the track invasion afterward are genuinely one of the loudest moments in world sport. For 2026 the race is scheduled for September 4-6, so this is peak late-summer Lombardy — warm, long days, and the surrounding vineyards and lakes still in season.

## Getting there

Monza sits about 15-20 km northeast of Milan, and most visitors base themselves in Milan and day-trip in. From Milano Centrale or Milano Porta Garibaldi, regional trains (Trenord) reach Monza station in roughly 12-15 minutes. From Monza station, dedicated race-weekend shuttle buses run to the circuit gates; alternatively it's a walkable 25-30 minute route through Parco di Monza for those who don't mind the crowds and want to see the park itself. Milan's two airports, Malpensa and Linate, connect to the city by train or bus, and Bergamo Orio al Serio is a viable low-cost alternative about an hour away. If arriving by car, expect the surrounding roads to be gridlocked on race day; official park-and-ride lots are usually signposted well outside the immediate circuit zone — using them beats trying to park near the track itself.

## Tickets and what to expect on-site

Grandstand options range from the high, panoramic Curva Grande and Ascari stands to the general-admission Prato (infield lawn) areas, which are cheaper and let you roam between viewing points across the weekend. The Rettifilo grandstands along the main straight put you closest to the podium and pit lane action. Because Monza is one of the best-attended rounds of the season, tickets for the main grandstands tend to sell out months in advance — buying through the official Formula1.com ticketing partner or the Monza circuit's own site as soon as the 2026 window opens is the safest route, and it's the only reliable way to confirm current prices, gate times, and support-race schedules, none of which should be assumed from prior years. A three-day pass covering Friday practice, Saturday qualifying, and Sunday's race is the standard format; single-day tickets are usually also available but Sunday sells out fastest.

## What to see and do beyond the race

Arrive early enough on any day to walk the fan zones, which typically include driver appearances, team merchandise stands, and demo runs. Inside Parco di Monza, the Villa Reale di Monza (a grand 18th-century royal residence) and its gardens are worth a wander if you need a break from the noise. The circuit itself retains ghostly banked curves from the old high-speed oval built in the 1950s, visible from parts of the infield — a good spot for photos away from the main grandstand crowds. In Monza town, the Duomo di Monza houses the Iron Crown of Lombardy, a relic tied to Charlemagne, and makes for an easy half-day detour if you're staying multiple nights.

## Practical tips

Pack for sun and heat — early September in Lombardy regularly hits the high 20s°C, and grandstands offer little shade. Bring earplugs; even from general admission areas the sound of an F1 field passing at over 340 km/h down the Rettifilo is startling at close range. Food and drink inside the circuit are available but queue-heavy and pricier than town, so many visitors bring a packed lunch for practice days. Hotels in Monza itself book out fast and at a premium; staying in Milan and commuting by train is often cheaper and just as fast. Finally, treat the September 2026 date as provisional until confirmed on the official F1 calendar closer to the season, since race weekends occasionally shift by a week.
