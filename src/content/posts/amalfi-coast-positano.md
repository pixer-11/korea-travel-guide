## Why Positano

Positano is the postcard image of the Amalfi Coast: pastel-colored houses stacked vertically on a cliffside above the Tyrrhenian Sea, terraced with lemon groves and connected by steep staircases instead of flat streets. Unlike Amalfi or Ravello, Positano has no single dominant monument — its appeal is the town itself, the way it tumbles toward the water around the dome of the Church of Santa Maria Assunta, whose 13th-century Byzantine icon of a "Black Madonna" is said to have given the town its name. John Steinbeck's 1953 essay for Harper's Bazaar, written after a stay here, helped put Positano on the international map and its lines are still quoted on plaques around town.

## Getting there

There's no train station in Positano — the nearest railway stops are Naples or Salerno, from which most visitors continue by road or sea. The classic approach is the SITA Sud bus along the SS163 Amalfitana coastal road, connecting Positano with Sorrento, Amalfi, and Salerno; buy tickets before boarding at a tabacchi or bar, since they aren't sold onboard. Between roughly April and October, ferries and hydrofoils run from Sorrento, Amalfi, Salerno, Naples, and Capri directly into Positano's small harbor — often faster and far more scenic than the bus, though seas can cancel sailings in bad weather. Driving is possible but discouraged: parking is scarce and expensive, and the coastal road is narrow with switchbacks and heavy summer traffic. If you do drive, expect to leave the car in a paid garage near the top of town and walk down.

## What to see and eat

Positano has two main "streets," both really staircases: Via Cristoforo Colombo and Via dei Mulini, which wind down through boutiques, ceramics shops, and the linen-and-sandal stores the town is known for. They lead to Spiaggia Grande, the main beach, lined with cafés and beach clubs renting loungers and umbrellas. From the harbor, boats and short walks reach Spiaggia di Fornillo, a quieter beach just west, and pathways continue toward the dramatic Fiordo di Furore. Right on Spiaggia Grande, the Church of Santa Maria Assunta is worth ten minutes for its maiolica-tiled dome and the icon inside. For food, look for scialatielli ai frutti di mare (a thick hand-cut pasta with seafood), fresh anchovies, and the lemon everything — granita, sfusato amalfitano candied peel, and delimoncello. Restaurants along the beach and up the staircases range from simple trattorias to higher-end terraces with sea views; a coffee or spritz with a view costs a premium here, which is part of the transaction.

## When to go

Positano is at its best in late spring (May–June) and early autumn (mid-September to early October), when the sea is warm enough to swim, restaurants and boat services are fully running, but the crowds and heat of July–August have eased. August itself brings intense heat, packed beaches, and inflated prices, plus Ferragosto (August 15) crowds. Winter is quiet — many hotels, restaurants, and boats close between November and March/April, so it's a poor season for a first visit despite the lower prices. Whatever the month, arrive early morning or late afternoon for photos of the town from the water or from the Sentiero degli Dei ("Path of the Gods") trail above, since midday light flattens the famous vertical view.

## Nearby & tips

Positano works well paired with Praiano, Amalfi, and Ravello along the same coastal bus/ferry route, and with Capri via a short hydrofoils hop. Wear real shoes — the town is essentially one long staircase system, not flat, and heels or thin sandals become impractical fast. Book accommodation and any beach club loungers ahead in high season, and confirm current ferry timetables and SITA schedules locally, since both shift by season and weather.
